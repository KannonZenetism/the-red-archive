# Pagan Yahwism: Archaeology of the Religions of Ancient Israel

**Gerett Treas**
RGS410-01

---

Most people in the West are familiar with the Holy Bible and its teachings, but it is worth considering that there may be an alternative story — one backed by empirical evidence. The Old Testament has long been regarded as the "Word of God," inerrant and infallible. Archaeologists have challenged that view and posed new difficulties for ancient traditions, uncovering extra-biblical information about the origins of the Israelite people, their culture, and their religion. Alongside the monotheism of the Bible, a pagan folk religion was present in Israel long before the formation of the official state religion and its accompanying cultural apparatus. An investigation of pagan Yahwism reveals a fuller record of the Israelites' development — of Yahweh's consort, Asherah, and of evidence in the form of masseboth, bamot, and clay or terracotta figurines. This paper seeks to draw that data together to provide a clearer understanding of early Israel and the diversity of its religion.

Little known in the West — strangely, for a region so pervaded by Judeo-Christianity — ancient Israel actually had two religions: the "folk religion" and the "official religion," otherwise known as the "state religion" or the "Book religion" (Dever, 2005, p. 5). Two distinct dimensions are present within these divided segments of Israel's religion, for the "state" and "folk" religions each had their own features. The state religion emphasized text, belief, ideology, and public worship, whereas the folk religion stressed artifact, practice, action, and private worship. The list of distinctions is long; but even the simple mention of differences in belief, practice, ideology, and action reveals undeniable variance in religious life. The state religion, in short, commits its adherents to belief and ideology, whereas the folk religion takes an engaging approach, calling people to do things for themselves (Dever, 2005, p. 5).

Recent scholars of Israelite religion have taken part in a modern trend, redirecting their focus away from the assumption of a single authoritative faith and a sole deity. They now read "Yahweh and (his) Asherah" in the material culture of Kuntillet 'Ajrud and other sites that deviate from the common Yahwistic picture, leading more scholars to think it probable that multiple religions existed side by side in ancient Israel. Perpetually changing, these religions were influenced internally and externally by the political, economic, and cultural forces of the region. At Kuntillet 'Ajrud, Yahweh and his consort Asherah had children, and together they formed a divine council worshiped in ancient Israel. Aware now of the presence of multiple deities, students of the Bible are urged to pierce the veil created by the Deuteronomists and the priestly redactors in order to recover the reality of religious pluralism (Hess, 2007, p. 14).

Students of Israelite religion should recognize that this issue can never be reduced to face value. Giving written texts the final word can no longer be sustained in a world of expanding evidence; alternative answers and methods must be sought. The ideal measure would be to employ a good deal of skepticism, though not to the point of narrow-mindedness. Bible scholars are aware that the works gathered in the Hebrew Bible were compiled long after the events they describe. The Bible is said to have been produced by "composite writers" and "editors" through a complex literary process lasting more than a thousand years. One of the most prominent factors in its composition is the bias woven in by the orthodox nationalist parties. Furthermore, the Bible contains miraculous and legendary elements that strain the modern reader's credulity. These elements have increased doubt about the inerrancy of the Bible, as well as its historical and archaeological accuracy (Dever, 2003, p. 1).

The early twentieth century saw a debate between the fundamentalists and the modernists over such issues as the authorship, date, context, and authenticity of the biblical texts. Fundamentalists and laypeople were suspicious of what they took to be the modernists' intent to "discredit" the works of the Bible, but this was not the modernists' aim; they meant to convey a more coherent "history of events" to the religious community. As it happens, the Exodus and the Conquest were the edifice upon which the whole of scripture was erected. These accounts were formative to the history of the Israelites, the people of the Bible, but they also shaped the American Revolution and the distinctive American experience and sense of destiny (Dever, 2003, p. 4).

In investigating the origins of the history of the Bible, attention must be directed to the "Exodus" and the "Conquest," two hot topics in Syro-Palestinian archaeology. The Exodus and Conquest concern the origin of the people of the Bible, the Israelites. It has been asked, "If the Exodus account is a myth, what then may we believe?" Some archaeologists hold that their discipline cannot fully illuminate the events at the origin of Israel. Much depends on interpretation; but interpretation should not be abandoned, since archaeology is the only extra-biblical source on the subject, aside from the "Israel Stele" of Merneptah (Mazar, 1990, p. 354). The story of the Israelites and their establishment in the land of Canaan begins with the Exodus from Egypt. This event marks the beginning of the nation of Israel, as the biblical account renders it in Exodus, Leviticus, and Numbers, and the material composes one-seventh of the "history" of Israel from Genesis to 2 Kings (Dever, 2003, p. 7). The date of the Exodus is now believed to fall in the late thirteenth century B.C., rather than the fifteenth century B.C. once supposed (Dever, 2003, p. 8).

The Bible is both a product of, and the only written source for, Israel's United Monarchy; it is therefore considered the basis of our historical information about that period. Eventually the tribal structure of the Judges became unsuitable for Israel, and so Saul, of the tribe of Benjamin, became Israel's first king. During Saul's reign, the Israelite tribes of the north — Gilead and Judah — were joined into a unified political body. After Saul's death, David became king in Hebron, recognized as such by Judah and various tribes of Israel. David ruled from c. 1000–965 B.C. Following the takeover of Jebusite Jerusalem, the city was recognized as the "City of David" and became the seat of David's dynasty, which continued through a succession of royal houses for more than four hundred years. The centralized state of Israel was completed under Solomon, son of David, who reigned in a time of great economic wealth and administrative reorganization, with a far-reaching commercial network of trade with nations such as Cilicia, Egypt, and Arabia (Mazar, 1990, pp. 369–371).

Some of the most striking information about the Hebrew Bible comes from knowledge of how the biblical compilers worked. Many people see the fluency of the Bible as evidence of God's hand in its compilation; nonetheless, scholars know that the different "strands" in its literary tradition — such as the two major works of the Pentateuch, in combination with the Deuteronomistic history — have been skillfully woven into a whole through the writing and editing of a group of anonymous authors. The sources for the Pentateuch come from the "J school" (representing Yahweh, or Jahweh) and the "E school" (derived from God's other name, Elohim). The J school's work is dated to near the tenth century B.C., and the E school's composition to near the ninth. The two were fused in the eighth century, forming a third and final school, the "P school" (the Priestly school), which edited the material of both sources into the work we now possess in the Hebrew Bible. For all that, the Pentateuch (or the Tetrateuch — the Pentateuch minus Deuteronomy) and the Deuteronomistic history were not committed to writing until some five hundred years after the events they set out to convey (Dever, 2003, p. 8).

Inevitably, archaeologists conclude that between foreign pagan practice and pure Yahwistic monotheism there existed a cult most accurately termed pagan Yahwism. The monotheistic cult centered on the Temple at Jerusalem was ever present, promoted by its priests and prophets. Hezekiah and Josiah made special efforts to centralize Yahwistic monotheism in Jerusalem, but the archaeological data force us to conclude that they were less than completely successful. Judah was destroyed by the Babylonians and the Israelite monarchy ended in 586 B.C., yet Yahwistic paganism was regularly expressed in Jerusalem, let alone the rest of Judah (Stern, 2001b).

The Bible would have us believe that ancient Israel was entirely monotheistic. For some Israelites this was the case, especially those associated with the Temple in Jerusalem. The archaeological evidence, however, indicates that the Israelites' version of monotheism was anything but pure. For many, the national deity Yahweh was not the only god. A common belief among the Israelites was that Yahweh had a consort — a female partner named Asherah. Israelites also invoked this divinity with the help of images, figurines in particular (Stern, 2001b). Yahweh, the god of Israel, was originally conceived as a desert god. In keeping with the theophany at Sinai, many biblical references connect Yahweh with the desert: "Yahweh came from Sinai; He shone upon them from Seir; He appeared from Mount Paran" (Deuteronomy 33:2). Judges 5:4–6, Habakkuk 3:3, and Psalms 68:8–9 may also be cited, all of which refer to desert locations (Avner, 2001, p. 39).

Although the chief god of the Israelites was Yahweh, other nations had their own national deities. The Phoenicians' high god was Ba'al. Dagon was the chief god of the Philistines, though he was later supplanted by Ba'al. Milkom was the chief deity of the Ammonites, and for the Moabites the head god was Chemosh. The Edomites revered the high god Qos. Likewise, the Israelites and Judahites served Yahweh. All but the Edomite Qos are mentioned in the Bible (1 Kings 11:5, 7, 33); Qos, of course, does appear in the archaeological record. Each of these chief gods had assorted names, but the consort of these gods is nearly always the same: Asherah, Ashtoreth, or Astarte (Stern, 2001b).

Bull imagery serves as an excellent way to illustrate the similarities between the god of Israel and other Iron Age deities. The bull may function as a connector between Yahweh, the god of Israel, and Ba'al, the storm god of the Canaanite religion. The bull was in fact the accompanying animal of Ba'al in Canaan, and later Syria, where he was depicted standing on the back of a bull. In the religion of the northern tribes of Israel, the bull was thought of as a symbol of Yahweh, or as his pedestal, relating bull imagery to the cherubim of the Temple in Jerusalem. Bronze bull statues are found in Canaanite culture and were commonly made in Canaanite workshops located in cities such as Beth-Shean and Megiddo, where numerous metalworking industries existed in Iron Age I (Mazar, 1990, pp. 351–352).

There is, plainly, little difference between Yahweh and the other national deities of the Iron Age. As Richard S. Hess argues, "It is, in fact, no longer possible to accept a simple division between those who worshiped Yahweh as a single and unique deity, on the one hand, and those who served Baal and a pantheon of deities, on the other. Yahweh has now become a member of the pantheon of Iron Age Palestine" (Hess, 2007, p. 13). This revelation is mainly the result of a revolution in our understanding of Israelite belief during the monarchy (c. 1000–586 B.C.), and of the ninth/eighth-century B.C. inscriptions from Kuntillet 'Ajrud in the northern Sinai, which have been interpreted as mentioning "Yahweh and his Asherah," where Asherah is understood to be a goddess. The texts found at Kuntillet 'Ajrud, dated to c. 800 B.C., mention Yahweh in partnership with Asherah, the West Semitic goddess. What followed was a rethinking of religious practice in ancient Israel. No longer could Israel's religion be divided between those who served Yahweh and those who revered other gods; indeed, the Israelites worshiped Yahweh and other deities at the same time and in the same place (Hess, 2007, p. 67).

The goddess and chief god's consort of each nation was essentially the same, the differing factors being name and nation. The various nations used similar cultic objects — incense altars of stone and clay, censers of bronze and clay, incense burners and cultic stands, chalices, goblets, and bronze and ivory rods covered in pomegranates. Cult vessels were interchangeable, one deity capable of being supplanted by another for the same purpose without detection — quite a regular occurrence. Evidence of this can be found in a ninth-century B.C. stela erected by Mesha, king of Moab, who described himself as the "son of Chemosh" and who defeated the Israelites (also seen in 2 Kings 3:4–27). He also boasted, "[I] took t[he ves]sels of Yahweh, and I hauled them before the face of Chemosh" (Stern, 2001b).

Ugaritic texts mention Asherah in the Semitic pantheon; there she is known as Asherah of the sea, consort of El, the head of the pantheon. She was the creator of the gods and their mother. Asherah appears over forty times in the Hebrew Bible, though many of these mentions refer to a wooden pole or a living tree, which some take to stand in opposition to the goddess. There are five instances in the Bible where Asherah unmistakably refers to a goddess (1 Kings 15:13; 1 Kings 18:19; 2 Kings 21:3, 7; 2 Kings 23:3; 2 Kings 23:7); in other references the meaning is unclear (Ahituv, 2006, p. 64). A noteworthy point is that the Bible never mentions Asherah's having a temple, as one might expect of a goddess — likely the work of the monotheistic compilers (Ahituv, 2006, p. 65).

In the later mythos of mystical Judaism, the Kabbalah, Lilith — a night goddess and ghostly demon — is sometimes the consort of male gods, and she has her counterpart in another female figure, Shekinah. In Kabbalah, Shekinah came to represent the divine presence of God on earth. This idea derives from the name of Yahweh and from his cultic presence dwelling in the tent of the tabernacle in the wilderness; *mishkan* comes from the same root as Shekinah and means "to dwell." Likewise, "Lady Wisdom" — Hebrew *hokma*, Greek *Sophia* — can be found in numerous verses of the biblical text (Job 28:12–28; Proverbs 1:20–33; 3:7–19; 8:1–36; 9:1–18). In these instances "Lady Wisdom" appears as Yahweh's partner in creation; she is seen alone, speaking on Yahweh's behalf; she brings particular blessings of long life; and the Bible claims that "she is a tree of life to them that lay hold upon her" (Proverbs 3:18), supporting the view that the tree or wooden-pole interpretation of Asherah does not imply a meaning other than the goddess. Shekinah appears often in later Jewish sources — the Apocryphal books, Philo of Alexandria, the targums, and the Talmud. By the third century A.D. she had come to be known as a separate entity from God, the "Holy Spirit." The full doctrine of the Shekinah comes to fruition in the Zohar, the Kabbalistic "Book of Splendor." In such Kabbalistic thought, God is called "Father," or simply "Y" for Yahweh, while the feminine is called "Mother," also known as "Supernal Mother," "Matronit," "Shekinah," and, in various writings, "Bride." These are all later developments of the figure of Asherah, the consort of Yahweh (Dever, 2005, pp. 301–302).

It is not the case that the erecting of Solomon's Temple in Jerusalem restricted Yahwistic worship to a single sanctuary in ancient Israel. The reforms of King Hezekiah late in the eighth century B.C. (2 Kings 18:1–8; 2 Chronicles 29–31) and of King Josiah late in the seventh century B.C. (2 Chronicles 34–35) both attempted to centralize Yahwistic worship at the Temple in Jerusalem. That such reforms were necessary is itself proof that the worship of Yahweh was not confined to Jerusalem (Stern, 2001b). The Bible indicates that there were sanctuaries of Yahweh at Dan, Shechem, Shiloh, Bethel, and Beersheba, and archaeology confirms that many other sanctuaries beyond Jerusalem were erected to the national god of Israel. Yahweh received a dedicated cultic installation at Judahite Nebo in Moab in the ninth century B.C. Mesha claimed in his stela that he had taken vessels of Yahweh from Nebo, which he later employed at a Moabite sanctuary dedicated to the god Chemosh. Cult installations have been excavated at numerous fortresses of Israel and Judah. Vered-Jericho was one such site, which its excavator, Avi Eitan, called a bamah, or high place (Stern, 2001b).

The archaeological record for the United Monarchy is somewhat lacking, sometimes controversial, and provides no clear-cut answers to the questions most often asked (Mazar, 1990, pp. 369–371). Archaeologists find it difficult to discuss material culture when it is absent from the literary record — for instance, when cult or ritual equipment is discovered by excavation in Palestine and Israel but goes unmentioned in the Hebrew Bible. As one archaeologist puts it, "This is to be expected from accounts written by religious reformers who were describing the Israelite religion they advocated rather than as practiced in their time. Moreover, not only is the account idealistic, it is elitist in its concern with established religion not with the religious practices of the majority of the population within their families and their homes" (Moorey, 2003, p. 1).

In the Hebrew Bible the term *masseboth* is used to describe "pillars," or "standing stones." These stones were set up vertically in the ground, either alone or in groups, and are plentiful in the deserts, where Israel first became a "God-fearing nation" — or, perhaps more accurately, a "Yahweh-fearing nation" (Avner, 2001, p. 31). Pairs and triads of masseboth are the most common groupings found. They may range from only a few inches in height to six feet or more. They often face east, and many have a circular compartment or cell at their base. Other features include offering benches, altars, and basins (Avner, 2001, p. 32).

The earliest masseboth of the Near East were found in the Negev and the southern Jordan deserts and date to the tenth and eleventh centuries B.C. Masseboth were extremely common between the sixth and third centuries B.C. and were erected throughout the biblical period and beyond. They are much less common, however, in the non-desert areas of the Near East — most notably at prehistoric sites, where their numbers increase significantly only during the second century B.C. (Avner, 2001, p. 32).

Biblical and extra-biblical literature refers to two kinds of masseboth: those that represent the gods and their abodes, and those that represent the spirits of ancestors. Archaeology confirms both kinds, and many peoples of the world still erect stones of the latter type to their ancestors today. The former type is evidenced in the Bible by the account of Jacob at Beth-El. Waking from his dream of a ladder ascending to heaven, Jacob takes the stone that had been his pillow and sets it up, declaring, "This stone that I have set up as a pillar (massebah) shall be God's house" (Genesis 28:22; Avner, 2001, p. 33). Jacob likely believed the stone contained the power and spirit of God.

The common practice of placing the male massebah on the right and the female on the left can be connected to the biblical habit of placing male names before female ones — Adam and Eve, Ba'al and Asherah, and the like (Avner, 2001, p. 35). Mentions of masseboth are often directly correlated with the name of Yahweh and his cult. References of this kind survived the Deuteronomistic editing of the late seventh century B.C. and may be found in Deuteronomy, Joshua, Judges, Samuel, and Kings — this despite the fact that Deuteronomistic doctrine mandated the worship of Yahweh only in Jerusalem, with many strict prohibitions. Deuteronomy indicates that Yahweh hates masseboth (Deuteronomy 16:22). The apparent contradiction between the physical evidence and the textual record is rooted in the shifting cult and culture of the Israelites during the First Temple period (c. 960–586 B.C.). The blame should be placed not on Canaanite influence, but on a shared desert origin and a conflict of interest between the masseboth cult and the state-based Israelite religion (Avner, 2001, p. 39).

King Josiah conducted an aggressive religious reform against bamot, "high places." He also renewed the covenant between the people and Yahweh at an object called *amud*, meaning "pillar" (2 Kings 23:3; 2 Chronicles 34:31). The biblical treatment of bamot suggests that the Deuteronomistic editors did not want their readers to be aware of the bamot's function in Israelite and Judahite society. The biblical bamot (singular *bama*) are often considered to originate with Canaanite worship, even though the term is not associated with the sacred in Canaanite dialects. A bama may be understood as a platform or high place of Canaanite origin, frequently mentioned in biblical passages (Nakhai, 2001, p. 161). The view that the bama is of Canaanite origin has not gone unchallenged; excavators at the site of Tel Dan believe the bama may have originated as an Israelite concept (Nakhai, 2001, p. 162).

Until recently, four primary interpretations of bamot have been held: first, that they were primitive, open-air compounds found on hilltops, usually equipped with some assemblage of *asera* ("sacred pole"), *massebot* ("standing stones"), and altars; second, that they were artificially raised platforms upon which religious rites were conducted; third, that they were altars of sacrifice; and fourth, that they were mortuary installations. The explanation that bamot were elevated open-air cultic sites has been the most generally accepted (Nakhai, 2001, p. 162).

More often than not, the Hebrew Bible condemned bamot worship and called for the destruction of the high places. The Deuteronomistic compilers promoted the exclusive legitimacy of Jerusalem-centered worship by defaming any alternative. Nonetheless, the Hebrew Bible preserves another perspective, illuminated by the various settings of bamot in Israel and Judah. Israel's original places of worship were local; the Hebrew Bible describes Israelites worshiping at numerous open-air sites, among the many locations where the Ark was placed — including a domestic sanctuary (Judges 17:4–5), a cultic center (Judges 18:30–31), and a sanctuary at Shiloh. The earliest references to a bama date to the tribal period, or the time of the Judges. Religious rites at the bama of an unnamed city in the district of Zuph are described in 1 Samuel 9:11–25, and a procession of prophets descending from a bama at Bethel is found in 1 Samuel 10:5 (Nakhai, 2001, pp. 163–164).

Taken together, the archaeological evidence — references to Yahweh and Astarte (Asherah) in Judean inscriptions, and the many clay figurines unique to Judah — leads us to conclude that, between foreign pagan practice and pure monotheism, a great deal of the collected material belongs to what may appropriately be termed pagan Yahwism. "It includes remains of sanctuaries, bamot ('open sacred high places'), as well as altars and figurines and other vessels in use at these sanctuaries. There appears to be hardly any difference between the function of these artifacts in the Judaean cult and in those of other Palestinian nations. The only real difference is the name of the chief national god, which was Yahweh in Judah" (Stern, 2001a, pp. 208–209).

A good deal of the paraphernalia relating to pagan Yahwism is made of clay, for clay was the most plentiful material available to the largest number of people. Most noticeable in the clay figures are their anthropomorphic, zoomorphic, and inanimate forms. Following the traditional way of image-making in the Near East, ordinary people engaged in this practice for nearly ten thousand years. Figurines of clay appeared before pottery, and they continued to be made well after the time of the Old Testament (Moorey, 2003, p. 1).

Two significant eighth-century B.C. inscriptions were discovered in modern Israel, one in the mid-1960s and the other in the late 1960s. The first site was a burial cave, and the inscriptions were retrieved from a rock-cut pillar near Khirbet el-Qom, twelve kilometers west of Hebron. The second site's inscriptions accompanied drawings on a pottery jar discovered during excavations at a shrine in a fort gateway at Kuntillet 'Ajrud, fifty kilometers from Kadesh-Barnea, in the northern Sinai. Both inscriptions use the expression "Yahweh and his asherah," implying a close relationship between the Old Testament God and a well-known Canaanite goddess (Moorey, 2003, p. 3).

Handheld clay plaques depicting a full-frontal nude female, a type epitomized in Canaan, offer a glimpse into the popular rituals of women in Late Bronze Age Canaan. The same model was rejected by the people of Judah — and, to a lesser extent, by the Israelites — under the United Monarchy. Only since the turn of the last century have Bible scholars begun to admit the existence of evidence for an early goddess cult that was popular in ancient Israel. Female terracotta images were extremely common in the village settlements of the Near East, and they were overshadowed by state formation early on in ancient Sumer, before and after 3000 B.C. The new elites of that period used clay figurines — mostly males in chariots — to conceive of themselves and to depict their positions of power and authority. The female images of this period were not the models so common earlier in Sumer and in the Samarra and Ubaid cultures of later prehistory; rather, they became flat, stylized nude silhouettes of the female figure, featured with eyes but no mouth, much as in prehistory (Moorey, 2003, p. 25). Likewise, the terracotta female imagery of Egypt — the goddess Hathor, corresponding to Ishtar-Inanna of Mesopotamia — appealed to women and to their chief concerns in life (Moorey, 2003, p. 26).

Sha'ar Hagolan contains the vastest collection of prehistoric art in Israel, and in the world. The design and construction of its objects give this impression, as they appear either surreal or abstract in conception. Goddess, pebble, male, and animal figurines, along with basalt pebbles impressed with various geometric patterns, weights, and seals, were among the findings. The excavators identified eleven categories of figurines: (1) clay "goddess" statues, (2) seated clay "goddess" figurines, (3) pebble figurines, (4) clay pillar figurines, (5) cylindrical clay figurines, (6) other clay figurines, (7) other stone figurines, (8) animal clay figurines, (9) engraved pebbles, (10) decorated weights, and (11) seals (Garfinkel, 2004, p. 135). The unique demographics of Sha'ar Hagolan make it one of the most densely concentrated sites for prehistoric objects in ancient Israel (Garfinkel, 2004, p. 138). The purpose of the female figurines in the Neolithic period was the fertility of man, animal, plant, fruit, and soil; the "Mother-goddess" represents fertility in all its aspects. There were thus four different types of clay "goddess" figurine at Sha'ar Hagolan: clay figurines, statues of a seated woman, clay figurines of a crouching woman, and numerous variations carved of stone (Garfinkel, 2004, p. 149).

Yahwistic worship of the past differs from the modern interpretation, and there is a wealth of evidence that should serve to persuade others that this is so. The organization of Judaism into a monotheistic practice centered on a single national deity served as a political ploy to benefit those who stood to profit from the particular message they wished to convey. With the archaeological data in hand, sole reliance upon the writings of the people who passed them down in their present form is no longer necessary. Material evidence is available that sheds light on the biblical authors and their goals, giving archaeologists the ability to contrast what the compilers wrote with the daily lives and religious practices of the common early Israelites — and to reveal the disparities between them. The history of the origin of the Israelites is somewhat muddled, since much of the Exodus account, which supposedly treats Israel's origin, is myth; as an extremely complex issue, an adequate treatment of origins could fill a work of its own. Yahweh was certainly not unique in the sense the biblical authors render him, nor was he alone in governing the world, as the figure of his spouse Asherah shows. The material evidence of masseboth has displayed Yahweh's consort. Bamot have shown that places of worship condemned in the Hebrew Bible were often used by common Israelite people in the folk religion of ancient Israel. And terracotta figurines have served as excellent examples of goddess recognition — though not every female figurine need be a goddess, and context should always be examined. Pagan Yahwism is a broad and open topic, with much left to discover. There should be ample evidence available to convince even the most committed skeptic that this story is more than one-sided.
