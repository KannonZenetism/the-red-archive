# An Exploration of Art, Symbolism, and Architecture of Early Christianity

**Gerett Treas**
ARC395-01 / RGS395-01
2014

---

The Christian religion has always been rich with symbolism. Symbols are present within the written record — the Bible — which is more a historical concern than an archaeological one. Art predominantly conveys symbols through images, which may make up part of the archaeological data, and architecture plays a vital role in Christian artistic expression. This symbolism carries a language long lost — or at least overlooked and ignored — by the modern Christian era, and an understanding of that language is vital both to the religion itself and to the history of its origins and development. The purpose of this paper is to illustrate what these symbols, images, and structures mean, how they function within the context of the Christian religion, and what possible hidden messages they contain. Each symbol is, in its own way, definable. Clear yet subtle, these coded messages have gone incognito generation after generation — and yet the churches, which ought to be the vessels of their transmission, have forgotten their original meaning.

Art and symbolism flourished in the time of early Christianity. To place it on a contextual timescale, we might divide it into subcategories such as Byzantine, Romanesque, and Gothic, with substructures under the guise of rulers such as Constantine and Justinian. The legalization of Christianity by Constantine initiated many elaborate Christian undertakings. Before Constantine, there were no elaborately built churches; worship was communal, taking place in arena-like sites with no fixed hierarchy. Constantine himself expresses it thus:

> It will be well, therefore, for your sagacity to make such arrangements and provision of all things needful for the work, that not only the church itself as a whole may surpass all others whatsoever in beauty, but that the details of the building may be of such a kind that the fairest structures in any city of the empire may be excelled by this….For it is fitting that the most marvelous place in the world should be worthily decorated. (Armstrong, 1993, p. 1)

Pagan symbolism existed in Christianity before Constantine, which is of notable importance. Symbolism is perhaps the most ancient language of all. In the modern world it is taboo for the religious to discuss the primordial roots of their practice, when it should instead be their most sincere desire to learn as much as they can about it. A great deal of the earliest Christian imagery has a Greco-Roman background. Christians began using certain symbols for the sake of converts from paganism, so that they would not feel estranged upon professing the new faith; syncretism aids conversion. Early Christians also did this because of the persecution they faced in a predominantly pagan society while holding beliefs considered unorthodox or outside established tradition. Because Christians were oppressed in early society, they had to avoid any symbol that might bring shame or harm upon them. One example is the cross, which one author describes by saying that "…the cross, now the universal symbol of Christianity, was at first avoided not only for its direct association with Christ but for its shameful association with the execution of a common criminal also" (Gough, 1974, p. 10).

Understanding the symbolic content within the early Christian literary and artistic traditions is of vital importance. Symbols, in this tradition and others, reveal much of the subtlety of human thinking. They are in essence a language composed of pictures — appearing simple, yet truly complex in application. Symbolism was not invented after the biblical events, though most symbols did not become externalized until the time of Constantine. Symbols are found within the biblical text, but circumstantial changes led to advances and new developments in this system of symbols. Most notably, the symbolic language is not only the key to understanding the pictorial or structural metaphor; it also serves to illuminate literary symbology within the biblical accounts. This, however, will have to serve as a future unification within academia.

Multiple symbols may be attributed to Christian thought. The peacock is one such symbol. It is neither well known nor widely used in modern Christianity, but in ancient times it was a symbol for Christ: "…the peacock with its supposed incorruptible flesh symbolized immortality" (Gough, 1974, p. 10). The power of symbols in the early Christian world was of the utmost importance, for symbols depict in simplicity many of the religion's most sacred truths. Some of the art following Constantine fused Christian and Roman pagan belief into constructed motifs. In the Tomb of the Julii in the Vatican, Christ is shown in the guise of Helios, driving a sun chariot across the sky. There are other examples as well, such as the Lateran Museum in Rome, where the vintage feast of the Dionysus cult serves as a reminder of Christ's vine and branches. The shepherd, a borrowed figure, accordingly carries the lamb across his shoulders; he is an adaptation of the Greek Hermes Criophorus, the ram-carrier, often depicted in sculpture.

The fish was a powerful symbol for early Christianity, for obvious reasons. The Gospel accounts often make reference to fish: Jesus's disciples are called to be fishers of men, and there was a symbolic feeding of the multitude with fish and bread that represented divine food from above, like the manna in the wilderness given to Moses and the Israelites. It is also believed that the fish is an acronym for Jesus Christ himself — Ίησοῦς Χριστός, Θεοῦ Υἱός, Σωτήρ — meaning Jesus Christ, Son of God and Savior (Gough, 1974, p. 10). A connection is often made between this and the Semitic cult of Dagon, who was also a fish god. Converts could easily have taken the notion of Dagon as part fish, together with the meal of fish, and drawn a direct association between that figure and Christ. This too would be a Christian incorporation of pagan symbolism — seemingly before any artistic depiction — and it would be consistent with the other adoptions of pagan imagery by Christian converts. In fact, it would make conversion more likely.

The cross was unusable, in terms of depiction or representation, by early Christians, so other things were used in its place. For example, "…the tau-cross which has no upright above the horizontal bar. The anchor, another cross substitute on epitaphs symbolized hope and ultimate security, while the Egyptian ankh (a tau-cross with a looped top) was close enough in form to be accepted as a cross by local Christians…" (Gough, 1974, p. 26). Today the cross is the most sacred symbol of the Christian religion, seen externally and internally on all official church buildings. Part of this shift comes from the fact that the cross later lost its connotation as a mark associated with a criminal. The cross had previously been a mark of shame and a point of considerable unattractiveness to potential converts, for it seemed wrong to follow a man who had died a criminal's death. As time progressed, Christians felt they could use the cross more freely, without fear of rejection, because it had lost that criminal connotation. But the criminal association was precisely the reason for the early adoption of pagan symbols, undertaken to avoid heaping further negativity upon their practice.

The anchor represents stability, or security, in the stormy seas of life. In difficult times one would be steadied by the weight of the anchor, so as to remain unshaken. There is no corresponding symbolic element among the pagans. It may also represent the reaching of port — in other words, arrival in heaven. Fish are often depicted alongside the anchor, signifying both believers and Christ. The anchor may appear to the Christian adherent as a trident, a further image of the cross; and if entangled with a dolphin, it can also depict the crucifixion.

Candles light the way for the soul on its journey between heaven and earth, the light showing that paradise is its goal. Along with candles, torches were used by pagans and Christians alike in their burial rituals. The Alpha and the Omega were symbols of the first and the last, since they are the first and last letters of the Greek alphabet, and they were often seen in combination with the cross and the monogram. This was especially true around the third and fourth centuries, when particular Catholic beliefs were being threatened by the Arians; the Catholics held that the affirmation of the Alpha and Omega was a confirmation of Christ's divinity.

The serpent served as a common symbol for the early Christians. Its obvious negative association with Genesis made it a prime candidate for the personification of evil. At the same time, it should be recognized that during the wanderings of the Israelites in the wilderness, Moses lifted up a bronze serpent that saved the lives of Israelites suffering from snakebite. Christ would later compare himself to this serpent in the canonical Gospels: the lifted serpent was a foreshadowing of Christ being lifted up on the cross, for as the serpent was lifted in the wilderness, so was the Son of Man. Jesus also told his followers to be gentle as doves but wise as serpents, which seems to indicate that the serpent is not a solely evil figure, since here it is associated with wisdom (Milburn, 1988, p. 4).

Other animals — the deer, hind, hart, dove, and lamb — were prominent early Christian symbols. The deer, hind, or hart represents a potential Christian convert desiring to receive baptism or to take part in the Holy Sacraments. The dove is a well-known symbol of innocence and further represents baptism and salvation; a parallel can be drawn between Noah's release of the dove from the ark and the future event of baptism. The dove is often depicted with an olive branch, suggesting that the soul is received through baptism. Lastly, the lamb can be seen as synonymous with Christ. The lamb is representative of innocence and meekness; it also stands for the faithful, and may specifically symbolize the twelve Apostles.

Christian art arises out of a period in the Roman Empire when its decay seemed all but inevitable. Military reforms were issued by Diocletian and Constantine to ensure the empire would endure, even as they transformed it into a centralized autocracy. This autocracy eventually did away with the old Roman order, giving way to the Byzantine Empire. The Christian religion had an advantage over many other religions of its time, and the reason was twofold: it had a monotheistic claim to rule the world, made manifest in the divine right of the king (first seen with Constantine in the fourth century), and its hierarchical structure, as developed in Rome, modeled the hierarchy of the Roman state. As one writer puts it, "The Christian religion, which, like countless other salvationist Eastern faiths, had swept the West in the chaos of the 3rd century, had two advantages over them: the exclusivity of its monotheistic claim to rule the world and an episcopal constitution whose hierarchical organization was modelled on Rome" (Hutter, 1971, p. 7). The emperor thus became God's divine representative on earth, even as the foundations of primitive Christianity crumbled beneath the weight of imposing state sponsorship and established orthodoxy.

Early in the third century, "official" art began to show the influence of preexisting Roman folk art and of the art prevalent in the Eastern provinces, with its particularly oriental style. Christian art in the third century introduced new artistic ideas; the art is described as "highly anti-organic, geometrical, and expressive" (Hutter, 1971, p. 10). Works of this kind were inaugurated around 300 AD. Moving into the time of Constantine, Christian art became much more refined, and it is said that under Theodosius I, around 400 AD, a unified approach was reached. Two intellectual tendencies determined the nature of early Christian art: tradition — a reliance upon an ancient art that was being diluted and transformed — and renovation, the conscious revival of it.

Iconography became prevalent during the early period, and themes were likewise devised. The manner of the images was predetermined, before their composition, by the use of preset themes. This mode of organization took place mainly in the fourth and fifth centuries, and painting became more predominant in this period as well, with pictographic depictions eventually coming to cover church walls and vaults. The mosaic, light-reflecting, gold, and multicolored, became representative of the non-mundane, or otherworldly, and supposedly related to the transcendental experience of the observer. The panel painting also became popular in this period. Written sources indicate that Christ, along with his apostles and saints, was depicted in the portraiture tradition of antiquity, a form of mummy portraits, though there is no archaeological evidence of this until the sixth or seventh centuries. Portrayals of Christ as the Good Shepherd are among the oldest images that exist — and this, obviously, is a subtly pagan concept.

The art of Christianity is more recent than Christianity itself, insofar as the early traditions rejected all pictorial depictions of anything pertaining to the faith. This view arose in light of the perceived idolatry of the pagans. Not only artistic depictions but also sacred edifices — namely, churches — were nonexistent. Only with Constantine do churches as elaborate structures begin to be built. In the early church, believers themselves made up the earthly temple, as the body of the faithful and of Christ; none of this depended on physical churches whatsoever. In the third century, more pictures were created and came to be regarded as instructive in the faith and supportive of preaching, and in this period a more appreciative attitude toward art developed.

Commonly depicted are scenes of the heavenly hopes of the faithful. These incorporate such symbols as the peacock for resurrection, the fish for Christ, and depictions of the Good Shepherd to convey their meaning. Other scenes began to be drawn into the art from the New Testament after the third century — baptism and the Eucharist being good examples, along with Daniel trapped in the lions' den, the swallowing of Jonah by the whale, the feeding of the five thousand, and the restoration of Lazarus to life. All were popular scenes of representation. In the fourth century, scenes from the Apocrypha began to be incorporated. The Passion of Christ, surprisingly, was not illustrated until the fourth century.

The ornaments of the church were determined by programs of theology. Dominion and Final Judgment were popular themes, along with Christ's Incarnation and his victory over death; Ecclesia and the Eucharist also received primary attention. The themes were always varied and multilayered in meaning. Talk of a "symbolic canon" arose, which has been described thus: "A symbolic canon evolved—the Cross and Christogram in the laurel crown; the Lamb of the Apocalypse on the Hill of Paradise, with the twelve apostle-lambs; the hand reaching out of the clouds, symbolizing God the Father; the dove representing the Holy Ghost; the throne prepared for Judgment (Etoimasia); and various others" (Hutter, 1971, p. 14).

Paintings of the Good Shepherd often show him among trees and flowers, in the company of sheep, with figures whose arms are raised surrounding him. Such a scene is meant to depict paradise — a divine place where the sheep are led to better pastures by their Good Shepherd. The sheep are the innocent, though the more dangerous and negative aspect of the sheep is to follow blindly, without question. Meekness can certainly be good, but, like all things, when misapplied it can become bad. The meek sheep is a follower; and how is it to know whether it follows the wrong shepherd, and how terrible that might be? They therefore need a truly "good" Shepherd to lead them home. The Shepherd certainly has elements of trust and innocence subtly built into its iconography.

Among the examples of early pictorial art is the Orans Figure, from the end of the third century. It is located in the catacombs of Priscilla, in the Crypt of the Velation Virgins in Rome. There are different depictions at this location, such as a teacher and a mother with her child, and the site is also one of the oldest cemeteries to have developed from a family vault. The style is similar to the early Tetrarchic art of 300 AD. Another example is the Good Shepherd. Dated to the mid-third century, it is located in the Catacombs of Callixtus in Rome and is one of the oldest examples of cemetery art in the Christian tradition. There are also many such depictions engraved on structures and buildings as part of the architecture.

The earliest Christian tradition rejected all pictorial representation of notions associated with the faith. For obvious reasons, the early Christians desired to separate themselves from what they saw as pagan idolatry. Along with this, the tradition denied itself a sacred structure — namely, the church — as a place of worship. It likewise rejected all outward representations as expressions of faith, even though the symbols were in truth already present within the written texts. The people who made up the tradition at this time were themselves the temple of God. This idea was also expressed by Paul, in his claim that believers are living stones in God's spiritual temple — in other words, that believers are the spiritual temple of the world. The structure is, symbolically, the Church as a spiritual edifice (Hutter, 1971, p. 8).

Christian architecture differs from the ancient pagan temple, notably in the idea that it is not only a house of God but also a place of worship. The symbolic components included in the structure — the altar, the priests, the faithful, and its being a place of sacrament and prayer attended by the witness of relics — were meant to supersede pagan architecture while retaining the ancient practice of building (Hutter, 1971, p. 8). In reality, all that changed was the way the temple was represented by its builders. The structure essentially plays the same role in its function toward God; the change comes from the way people interact with, and behave within, the structure. Adaptation and change of this kind parallel the changing tolerance for art and other forms of symbolism.

The first structure associated with Constantine was built in 313 AD and was called "the Lateran or Constantinian basilica in Rome" (Armstrong, 1993, p. 2). The basilica is built in the cathedral style, where bishops were seated and many medieval councils were held. This structure inaugurated the new recognition of the exalted status of the bishop in Rome; here all bishops received civil or judicial roles, and all churches acquired legal standing and function. Other similar locations include Aquileia, a double basilica in northern Italy; Trier, also a double basilica; and the palace aula, or basilica, at Trier, among others. All of these structures had a political or imperial function — a thing paramount to the state-mandated religion in its deviation from the ancient roots. The double basilica at Trier was built on imperial property, and the palace aula, or basilica, was a judicial complex.

A second type of early church associated with Constantine is the cemetery basilica, also known as the covered cemetery. There were not as many of these as of the former basilicas, and they were limited in location to Rome. The Church of San Sebastiano, also called the Church of the Apostles, may predate Constantine altogether. Some defining characteristics of this type of building are "the side aisle continuing as an ambulatory around the west end or apse, the circular west end itself, the adjacent mausolea, the presence of a catacomb, and—two features not shown in most drawings—the graves literally paving the floor of the nave and aisles and the site outside the city ramparts (extra moenia)" (Armstrong, 1993, p. 5). There is certainly artistic style involved in the creation of these basilicas; and though the meaning of their symbolism is a bit more obscure, their symmetrical layout and the time and precision lavished upon them indicate that their builders valued the particular way in which they were erected.

Imperial mausolea serve as a third category. Two types of these were built by two contemporaries of Constantine — one by Diocletian, the other by Galerius — with centralized tombs lying at the center of the palace complex in each man's chief residence. Diocletian's mausoleum, octagonal in form, was located at Spalato (Split), while Galerius's majestic circular tomb was at Thessalonica. The Anastasis Rotunda of the Church of the Holy Sepulcher is also related to this category of mausolea. In facilities of this type, Constantine preferred complex decoration and adornment — in keeping with the edict the emperor himself declared, demanding proper decoration for the most splendid place on the planet.

The palace church introduces a fourth category. These structures take a variety of forms, as varied as they are diverse. Antioch in Syria contains an Octagon, and Constantine's Hagia Sophia was represented by a double-sided basilica with corresponding aisles and galleries. One such palace church is Santa Croce in Gerusalemme, which is said to have become a palace church before 330 AD. In the 320s AD, these began to contain apses and cross arches, achieved by transforming existing reception halls into new arrangements.

A fifth type of church is similar to the category of imperial mausolea. Located in Rome and the Holy Land, this is the martyrium. St. Peter's shrine on Vatican Hill is the largest of the early Christian churches: its length was 295 and its inner length 390, and with a width of 210, there was also an atrium comparable in stature. There were mausolea about the walls from the fourth century, including an imperial tomb from 400. What is considered a great cross hall, consisting of a large apse, is the focal point for the assembly (Armstrong, 1993, pp. 2–13).

In light of all this, it is vital to understand that the art, symbolism, and architecture of early Christianity were a product of the progression of time. As they evolved, and as the religion was adopted by emperors, artistic expression became more common and the ancient traditions changed in favor of new conceptions. Simply to realize that the early Christians, before Constantine, did not erect sacred edifices and only vaguely projected their psychological profiles onto artistic assertions is extremely significant. The meaning of symbols is something that eludes the modern traditions, and it is only partly revealed in this study, as an explanation of what those symbols mean.

For the future, it would be interesting to consider how the various expressions of visual and architectural symbology relate to the textual imagery of the Bible, and what might be yielded from such an undertaking. What if the Bible were largely a work of symbology — of psychological expression, an exploration of consciousness, just waiting to be explored? What if metaphor and allegory in text are just as symbolic as actual visual representation? Such would be an extended and likely interdisciplinary endeavor, awaiting realization at some point in the future. For now, it is enough to know that symbolic knowledge does still exist; it has simply been pushed into the background, as so much of historical archaeology has been in favor of textual evidence. Perhaps when we overcome our disciplinary squabbles, more light will be shed on the literal and metaphorical interpretations of early Christian symbology.
