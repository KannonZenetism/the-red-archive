## Chapter One: Introduction

Describing the ultimate nature of reality is a difficult task — indeed, given the current state of human knowledge, it is likely an impossible one. Yet a lack of capability should never limit curiosity or the boundless vistas of imagination. Mathematics has long been a cornerstone of human civilization, evolving from a practical counting tool into the refined methodologies of the modern age; and remarkably, many of its procedures originated with philosophers, scientists, and theologians. It is no surprise, then, that these disciplines are indissolubly bound and complementary. Like the great polymaths of old, those who seek wisdom in the modern age must draw from the whole body of knowledge.

The concepts of zero and nothing have been, and still can be, profoundly menacing to ego consciousness. At first these ideas were merely impractical, but they then passed through many phases of denial and rejection, even causing tremendous angst within some of humanity's brightest minds. Countless brilliant individuals expended great effort attempting to disprove or suppress the concept of nothing, often to the detriment of those who dared to challenge the status quo. Eventually the fetters loosened and the concepts found their place, yielding both a wealth of new insight and no small controversy.

Infinity, likewise, has caused its fair share of vexation, though arguably less than zero. It has been especially troublesome in religious circles, where it provoked fears ranging from the possibility that it might be more powerful than God to the peril it posed to established tradition — most acutely if the extent of cosmological time were without restriction. Not all, however, have seen the infinite as a theological threat; many have viewed it as the sole domain of God and, like zero, ultimately unknowable. Two common claims among mathematicians are that infinity is not a real number and that it can never be pragmatically realized, only conceived as a potential to be approached yet never secured.

Science eventually came to embrace zero and infinity, though here too the very thought of "nothing" generated unrest. Many great discoveries followed from its adoption. Most notably, the void of a perfect vacuum is not empty; it is quite full, even as it remains less than nothing. In the Higgs field, nothing meets everything in the chaos of the infinite void. By the same token, black holes are null tears in spacetime, and yet, according to relativity, they are composed of infinite zeroes. Time may be limitless or eternal, so that the universe has neither beginning nor end — though this remains a mystery. Space, similarly, could be finite, but it may instead be infinite or boundless, such that one could never fully traverse it. These are compelling questions for physicists and cosmologists alike.

Philosophy and religion have often been closely intertwined; after all, philosophy was born from eager minds seeking answers outside the customs of their day. It follows that many of the arguments against zero, the void, and the infinite have come from philosopher-theologians — though not all of them. Some philosophers have argued for a literal, nihilistic definition of absolute nothingness. Many problems have arisen, chiefly around the concept of nothing, owing to a confusion of terms: it is inaccurate to call nothing a "something." Nothing is everything and it is nothing, but it is not any particular thing. Few philosophers have acknowledged zero, because the Greek mind failed to conceptualize it; on the whole, only the mystic-philosophers have grasped its full magnitude.

It is worth pausing to ponder the vast and unspeakable nature of existence, and to ask what people truly believe they know about the universe. Many suppose themselves well informed, and it is reasonable to want to be erudite and in control — but does this come at the cost of genuine knowledge? Why do some hold their traditions to be infallible, flawless, and perfect? To feel secure? It hardly matters. What matters now is that humanity begin to question the prevailing paradigm and grow humble enough to admit that the Absolute can never be pinned down or confined to a book or box of any kind. The nature of that Being is so expansive that it eludes finite perception; but this need not hinder inquiry, for one can always arrive at closer approximations of the truth. Herein lies the virtue of science: it is falsifiable, and therefore modifiable and adaptable.

This dissertation seeks to elucidate the archetype of the Absolute as nothing and everything, and to defend the position that an infinite nothing must be approached through a variety of disciplines, from a multitude of angles, if it is to be rendered cognizable. To that end the research draws on philosophy, science, theology, mathematics, history, and mysticism. It examines in detail the origins of zero and infinity, their implications for interdisciplinary study, and their relation to Absolute Being, along with the various positions taken by exceptional thinkers in their respective fields.

## Chapter Two: Review of Literature

Willis Barnstone and Marvin Meyer's anthology *The Gnostic Bible* (2009) compiles early Gnostic writings that explore the state of ultimate reality. It treats the concept of the Good, which the Gnostics held to lie beyond any god yet to remain intuitively recognizable, and which individuals could tap into to receive insight into a deeper reality. The book offers keys to understanding the vastness of existence. Barnstone is an American poet, memoirist, translator, Hispanist, and comparatist; Meyer was a professor of Christian Studies at Chapman University.

In *The Infinite Book: A Short Guide to the Boundless, Timeless, and Endless* (2005), John D. Barrow provides a comprehensive guide to the infinite, ranging from its origins in science and mathematics to modern research. Barrow observes that infinity, like zero, is one of the strangest concepts ever to enter the human imagination, and he probes the profound questions it compels us to ask. He is an English cosmologist, theoretical physicist, and mathematician.

In *You Are the Universe* (2017), Deepak Chopra and Menas Kafatos challenge the materialist paradigm, arguing that external reality mirrors the internal one — that outward objects in fact disguise the inward world of consciousness, the "real" actuality. They seek to reimagine the traditional scientific template of reality, holding that everyone is a co-creator and that the hidden dimensions of consciousness contain infinite possibilities. Chopra is an Indian-born American author; Kafatos is an American physicist of Greek descent.

In *Creation out of Nothing* (2004), Paul Copan and William Lane Craig defend the theological position that God created the world from scratch, out of absolutely nothing. They maintain that God is wholly "other" than the world and not immanent within it, marshaling biblical apologetics, philosophical argument, and scientific evidence in support. Copan is a theologian and analytic philosopher; Craig is an analytic philosopher and Christian theologian.

Raphael Demos's *The Philosophy of Plato* (1966) offers an interpretive analysis that aims to present Plato's thought as conveyed in his own texts. Rather than dwelling on later interpreters, Demos prefers to examine what Plato said directly. His chapter on reality — covering being, selfhood, and relatedness — was vital to the present research. Demos was a professor of religion and philosophy at Harvard University.

In *Archetype of the Absolute* (2017), Sanford L. Drob investigates the unity of opposites across historically disparate traditions, arguing that they are complementary. He frames the Absolute as a point of dissolution, a coincidence of opposites, and discusses traditions such as Taoism and Kabbalah alongside figures like G. W. F. Hegel and C. G. Jung. Drob is a philosopher and forensic psychologist.

Manly P. Hall's *Lectures on Ancient Philosophy* (2005) serves as a compendium of symbolic wisdom. Originally composed as a companion to his magnum opus, *The Secret Teachings of All Ages*, it contains a chapter on the nature of the Absolute that proved especially relevant to this project. Ultimately Hall concludes that the truly Real is blankness, space, or emptiness — something akin to nothingness or the void. Hall was a prolific author, mystic, and scholar.

Stephen W. Hawking presents a history of the known universe in *The Theory of Everything* (2002). Across seven lectures he explores the concepts of modern physics — the Big Bang, black holes, spacetime — and traces the history of ideas from Aristotle's contention that the Earth is round to Hubble's discovery of cosmic expansion. Hawking is widely regarded as one of the greatest theoretical physicists of all time.

Mike Hockney explores the creation of the universe in two related works. In *Ontological Mathematics* (2015), he explains creation from two ingredients — nothing and everything, zero and infinity — in accordance with the Principle of Sufficient Reason, which he argues exists ontologically as a mathematical necessity. His aim is to unseat scientific empiricism in favor of rationalism and idealism. In the companion work *Transcendental Mathematics* (2015), Hockney examines what he regards as Immanuel Kant's failure to explain science in a philosophical context, concluding that transcendental mathematics is the most practical way to make sense of the world. Hockney is a ghostwriter purportedly engaged by the Illuminati to reveal esoteric mysteries.

Jim Holt addresses the fundamental question of existence in two works. In his essay "Nothing ventured" (1994), he asks why something exists rather than nothing, drawing out the cosmological, ontological, and theological dimensions of the problem and questioning whether human beings can intelligently contemplate any topic at all before first understanding the reason for existence itself. Holt expands on these themes in *Why Does the World Exist?* (2012), a bridge between cosmology and philosophy in which he canvasses thinkers from many disciplines to ask why the universe exists when it would have been simpler for there to be nothing. Holt is an American philosopher and essayist.

Antonio Lamúa surveys the history of the infinite in *Secrets of Infinity* (2013), covering the scientific, philosophical, technological, and symbolic facets of the concept as illuminated by those diverse fields. Lamúa is an editor with a background in science.

Anna F. Lemkow's *The Wholeness Principle* (1995) is a theosophical treatise on the dynamics of unity. She discusses the principles of a unitive framework and the relationships among traditions that grapple with reality. Lemkow argues that scientific and logical traditions are limited insofar as they treat reality in purely descriptive terms; for her, actuality is ultimately indefinable. She held degrees from the University of Alberta and the New School for Social Research.

John Michell and Allan Brown explore creation myths through the lens of geometry in *How the World Is Made* (2012). They argue that the Creator made the universe a spherically shaped living creature, emanated from an ideal state, and they read traditional creation myths through the principles of sacred geometry. Michell was an esotericist; Brown is a professor and folklorist.

Stephen Mitchell offers a poetic rendering of Lao Tzu in *Tao Te Ching: A New English Version* (2006). The *Tao Te Ching* imparts balance, generosity, and a life lived according to the Tao, or Way. The Tao is the ground of being, the place where opposites are united in nondifferentiation before emerging into distinction; the concept is akin to a mathematical zero, in that one cannot speak about it directly. Mitchell is a poet and translator; Lao Tzu was an ancient Chinese philosopher.

Erich Neumann traces the evolution of the psyche in *The Origins and History of Consciousness* (2014). He discusses the uroboros, or World Parents, as the container of all existence in potential — pre-time and pre-space — providing a psychological framework for the unconscious origin of the world. Neumann was a psychologist and student of Carl Jung.

The dialogue between Buddhism and science takes center stage in *The Quantum and the Lotus* (2001), by Matthieu Ricard and Trinh Xuan Thuan, a Buddhist monk and an astronomy professor respectively. They show how Buddhism and quantum mechanics are complementary, and how the concept of emptiness parallels the quantum vacuum: a state that is not empty, yet is distinct from absolute nonexistence.

Charles Seife chronicles the history of a forbidden number in *Zero: The Biography of a Dangerous Idea* (2000). He recounts the origins of zero, the way it was reviled and then accepted over time, and its critical importance to mathematics, philosophy, science, and religion. Seife portrays zero as one of the greatest paradoxes in human thought. He is a journalist and professor at New York University.

Victor J. Stenger reviews the history of astrophysics in *God and the Multiverse* (2014). Stenger works to make complex ideas accessible while arguing that quantum-mechanical views of the multiverse are explicable in naturalistic terms, with no need for the supernatural. Stenger was a particle physicist and philosopher.

Terence Witt challenges standard cosmology in *Our Undiscovered Universe* (2007). Witt holds that "something" does not exist; only "nothing" does. He rejects traditional accounts of the universe's origin in favor of a cosmos that is infinite and eternal, with no beginning, end, or limit. Witt holds a BSEE from Oregon State University.

Finally, a panel at the World Science Festival titled *Pondering the Imponderables* (2018) gathered scientists, philosophers, and mathematicians to discuss the Big Bang, the multiverse, and the nature of infinity. The consensus was that science is fast approaching a discovery of how the universe works at its core. Yet the discussion also surfaced a tension: theorists increasingly proceed beyond what experimenters can verify, raising the question of whether a theory without empirical evidence retains scientific value.

## Chapter Three: Personal Relevance

Some time ago, a series of experiences awakened me to a deeper reality. I use the word "mystical" reluctantly, mindful of the stigma it carries; even so, the numinous character of those encounters is, for me, undeniable. I have changed considerably since then, and my beliefs and modes of understanding have shifted along the way. Yet I have always felt that presence with me — or, dare I say, of me — illuminating knowledge ordinarily sealed from the profane. At first I believed I understood it, and knew exactly the message I was meant to receive. I soon realized I was mistaken.

Through the study of various traditions — religious, philosophical, esoteric, and metaphysical — I came to recognize the essence of my realization in all of them. It became easier to see past words, to descend into the depths of an ideology and perceive its authentic face. As I understand it, that face is partly apprehensible yet at the same time unspeakable; it is experiential, not descriptive. I found the doorway to concepts like oneness and "none-ness" while investigating the Mystery schools, where the "ineffable nature of the Absolute" is a recurring theme.

As I see it, everyone is describing the same reality in different ways. The difference arises from language-based perception, which is inherently divisive. Viewed from the center, the good and the bad, the hot and the cold, the long and the short all become complementary. In the ground of being, opposites are necessary, for it is there that they are contained. There is much to be gained from recognizing this — above all, that one who is centered no longer needs to choose sides. This limits division without the need to fight against anything, since conflict only breeds further strife. As the old saying goes: when one does little, there is little to be done; when one does much, there is much to be done. Understanding this is of value to every metaphysician.

Researching this topic has helped me tremendously. I am grateful for the freedom to investigate what interests me most and what I believe is most beneficial to the world. The study has sharpened my knowledge in familiar areas and strengthened it where I lacked insight, clarity, and understanding — in mathematics and quantum mechanics, for instance. I am honored to have had the time to learn the history of these concepts, to witness the struggles between them across the centuries, and to behold the transition into the intellectual freedom we enjoy today. Now it is time to use that freedom to approach, with humility, what is truly Real.

## Chapter Four: Findings

### Nothing and the All

The concept of zero, or "nothing," is capable of threatening logic itself, and that inherent danger contributed to its marginalization by many early civilizations. Zero also lacked any practical use at first: no one goes to the store to buy zero bananas. When humanity first began to count, there was only "one" and "many." Over time this became one, two, and many; then one, two, three, and so on. Zero was simply not needed in daily life, for it seemed pointless to speak of a lack of something, and it did not occur to people that they might need to express the absence of objects at all. In prehistoric times, the ability to count was regarded as an arcane and mystical talent, akin to casting spells (Seife, 2000).

According to Seife (2000), zero first appeared in the East, in the Fertile Crescent of modern Iraq, by way of the Babylonian style of counting. The Greeks repudiated zero, and the Egyptians found no use for it despite being master geometers. The Babylonian system was sexagesimal, based on the number 60 — a curiosity, given that most other societies preferred 5, 10, or 20 as their foundational number. By roughly 300 BC the Babylonians had begun using slanted wedges to represent emptiness: a placeholder employed to mark which position a symbol occupied. In this way the Babylonians invented zero in order to give any sequence of digits a distinct and lasting meaning.

In mathematics, the name for nothing is zero, and its root is the Hindu word *sunya*, meaning void or emptiness. Jim Holt, in *Why Does the World Exist?* (2012), writes that "it was among the Hindu mathematicians that our notion of zero arose." For the Greeks and Romans the concept was unfathomable, since they could not imagine how "nothing" could be "something." Indian mathematicians, by contrast, were already familiar with emptiness from Buddhist philosophy. Zero was an abstract symbol denoting nothing, and so it took some time to be taken seriously by European mathematicians. At first it was more a punctuation mark than a number — until the rise of commerce changed that.

Eventually India's ascendancy was eclipsed by another Eastern civilization that rose as the West withered: Islam. In time Islam received zero from India, and the West in turn received it from Islam. Muslims came to reject Aristotle's vision of the universe in favor of his ancient adversaries, the atomists, who held that all matter is composed of individual particles called atoms, free to move about. If that is so, there must be a vacuum between them — and Aristotle detested the void. Zero brought the void back into contention. Christians, meanwhile, clung to Aristotle's vision and declared zero a heresy (Seife, 2000).

Zero reflected new teachings that controverted Aristotle's rejection of the infinite and the void. Muslims spread the notion throughout the lands under their authority, remaining all the while in conflict with the Aristotelian legacy. In the eleventh century, the Muslim philosopher Abu Hamid al-Ghazali decreed a death sentence for any who held to Aristotelian doctrine — which, reasonably enough, brought the debate to a close. It is fitting that the Muslims did not reject zero, for as Seife observes, "The Muslims, with their Semitic, Eastern background, believed that God created the universe out of the void — a doctrine that could never be accepted where people shared Aristotle's hatred of the void and of the infinite" (Seife, 2000). Where the Muslims embraced zero and abandoned Aristotle early on, the Jews followed close behind.

In due course Maimonides, a twelfth-century rabbi, came into conflict with Aristotelianism as he tried to reconcile the Eastern Semitic Bible with Western Greek philosophy. From Aristotle he learned to verify God's existence by denying the void, an argument that proved valuable to theology. Yet the Bible and the other Semitic traditions teemed with ideas affirming the reality of the infinite and the void. Some 800 years earlier, Saint Augustine of Hippo had likewise tried to reconfigure the Semitic Bible to fit Greek doctrine, and the early Christians chose to read portions of the Old Testament metaphorically. Maimonides, however, did not wish to Hellenize his religion completely. Bound by the rabbinic tradition, he had to accept the Bible's account of the void — which meant rejecting Aristotle (Seife, 2000).

The years after Maimonides's death became the age of nothing for the Jews. Kabbalism, or Jewish mysticism, began to spread in the thirteenth century. Central to Kabbalistic thought is gematria, the search for coded messages within the Bible; but Kabbalah was far more than number crunching — it was a tradition of profound mystical import, comparable to Hinduism. Kabbalah spoke of God's dual nature. The Hebrew term *ein sof* means "infinite" and represents God's creative aspect, which formed the universe and permeates every corner of it. The Hebrew term *ayin*, by contrast, means "nothing." The infinite and the void thus stand shoulder to shoulder as coequal aspects of the deity. *Ayin* is also an anagram of, and shares the same numerical value as, the term *aniy*, which means "I" in Hebrew. To the Kabbalists, God's encoded message was plain: "I am nothing, and I am everything" (Seife, 2000).

In 1277 Étienne Tempier, the bishop of Paris, convened a group of scholars to attack Aristotelianism. Tempier discarded the doctrines that conflicted with God's omnipotence — among them the claim that God cannot move the heavens in a straight line because doing so would leave a vacuum in their wake. God, he held, would permit a void if God so desired. Because of Tempier's effort the void was now allowed; an omnipotent deity need not abide by Aristotle's rules. Tempier did not abolish Aristotelian philosophy, but he wounded it gravely. The church would cling to Aristotelianism for a few more centuries, even as the shift toward the infinite and the void drew near. In the middle of the twelfth century the first translations of *Al-jabr* (Al-Khwarizmi's algebra) were entering Spain, England, and the rest of Europe. With perfect timing, just as the church was breaking the bonds of Aristotle, zero arrived (Seife, 2000).

The phrase *Ex nihilo nihil fit* means "nothing comes from nothing." It endows nothing with the power of self-generation, of being like God. Holt reports that Karl Barth, the twentieth-century Protestant theologian, believed nothing to be evil — that which God does not will (Holt, 2012). Indeed, in *Church Dogmatics* Barth wrote: "The character of nothingness derives from its ontic peculiarity. It is evil." For Barth, nothing rose up alongside "something" when God created the world. The two are a pair of ontological twins, of sorts, but of opposing moral natures. Nothingness is responsible for humanity's perverse streak, its tendency toward malice and its recoil from divine goodness. Barth regarded nothingness as Satanic.

As Holt (2012) writes, the existentialist philosopher Martin Heidegger, in his effort "to avoid saying, *Das Nichts ist* — 'Nothing is' — he was driven to an even more peculiar locution, *Das Nichts nichtet*: 'Nothing noths.'" Heidegger preferred to view nothing not as an inert object but as a dynamic, annihilating force. Robert Nozick, the American philosopher, pushed Heidegger's notion further still: perhaps nothing annihilated itself and thereby induced the observable world. If nothing could suck everything into nonexistence and hold it there, then perhaps it could suck nothingness into nothingness, and so produce everything. The existentialists, however, misunderstood the grammar of nothing, taking it to be an entity — a "something."

The notion that it is nonsensical to speak of nothing traces back to the origins of Western philosophy, and to Parmenides of Elea, the greatest of the pre-Socratics. Parmenides cast his argument about the nature of reality as an allegorical poem, of which only some 150 lines survive. In it an anonymous goddess presents the narrator with a choice between two paths: the path of being and the path of nonbeing. The latter is not real, because one can neither conceive of it nor speak about it. To dwell on nothing is to make no headway, and this limits existence; for if it is impossible to talk about nothingness, then one cannot even ask why something exists rather than nothing (Holt, 2012).

It helps to distinguish between nothing and nothingness. According to logicians, "nothing" is shorthand for "not anything." To say that "nothing is greater than God" is not to affirm some divine entity higher than the Supreme, but only that no particular thing is above God. "Nothingness," on the other hand, is a noun and an ontological option — a possible reality, an imaginable state of affairs. Some languages mark this distinction more clearly than others. Once the two are kept apart, the supposed paradoxes that arise in speaking of nothing become easier to settle — though the distinction is arguably unnecessary, since "no-thing" plainly indicates "not any-thing" (Holt, 2012).

Some philosophers have argued that absolute nothingness — the total absence of everything — is impossible, the very idea being self-contradictory. A simple solution to the problem of nothing is to say that there is something rather than nothing precisely because nothing is impossible: "There is just no alternative to being," as one philosopher put it. Henri Bergson, the French philosopher, tried to picture the annihilation of the universe and found, in the end, that something remained: his subjective self. He could not imagine a total void without the residue of consciousness lingering in the darkness. As Holt notes, "The British idealist F. H. Bradley, author of the dauntingly titled *Appearance and Reality*, similarly maintained that sheer nothingness was unthinkable. He too concluded that it must therefore be impossible" (Holt, 2012). But one may doubt whether something is impossible merely because it is unthinkable. Those who reason this way commit the philosopher's fallacy, mistaking a limit of imagination for a truth about ultimate reality. It amounts to saying, "I cannot imagine it otherwise; therefore it must be so."

Bergson and Bradley took total nullity to be a contradictory state of affairs, since to entertain the possibility seems to require an observer to contemplate it. It would not be contradictory, however, if "nothing" simply means "not anything." Their view implies that every possible world must contain at least one conscious observer. Perhaps one can imagine a universe without consciousness; perhaps, too, matter itself might be self-aware. Bergson managed to annihilate every aspect of his character but not his self, and so concluded that his own nonbeing was impossible and that he was a metaphysically guaranteed being, almost godlike in status (Holt, 2012).

A further argument holds that any effort to envisage nothingness is fated to be partial, never complete. Here the residue left behind is not consciousness but something nonpsychical. The container argument from nothingness runs as follows: when the cosmic contents are gone, no substance remains, yet the container — like an empty cupboard, like space itself — persists. Because a person cannot imagine the absence of this nothingness, it must be intrinsic to reality; it is necessary, like God. One might object that space is not a "something." On one view, the substantival, which traces back to Sir Isaac Newton, nullity is a real thing that would persist even if all its contents vanished. On the rival view, the relational, credited to Newton's adversary Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz — a universal genius and polymath — emptiness has no substantiality of its own; space is instead a network of relations among things, and depends upon them. The debate between the two endures to this day (Holt, 2012).

Philosophers known as metaphysical nihilists cling to the subtraction argument. As Holt (2012) shows, it begins by assuming that the world holds a finite number of objects — people, tables, chairs. It assumes further that all of them are contingent, so that any object that exists might not have existed. Lastly it assumes independence: the nonexistence of one thing does not require the existence of another. By subtracting contingent objects one by one, the argument concludes, one can imagine that nothing at all might have come to be. The finiteness and contingency premises seem acceptable, but the independence premise is questionable. Suppose only two objects remain — an electron and a positron, a "pair world." One might try to conceive of a singleton world in which only the positron exists. Yet this would violate the tenets of physics, in particular the law of conservation. The pair world's net charge is zero (the +1 of the positron and the −1 of the electron); a singleton world would carry only +1, and so would be a physical impossibility.

### An Infinite Dilemma

Because zero is innately bound up with the void, and so with nothingness, dread has often accompanied it. The fear of zero is a primal terror linked to void and chaos. Most ancient peoples held that the chaotic void preceded the ordered universe. Darkness was the mother who sprang from chaos, and the two together precipitated the rest of creation — the void being the zero-point energy of a quantum vacuum. The Hebrew tradition holds that the earth was chaotic and void before God brought forth the light and formed the world. The phrase *Tohu wa-bohu* ("without form and void") binds two primordial figures: *tohu* to Tehomot, the primal Semitic dragon whose body formed the sky and earth, and *bohu* to Behomot, the renowned Behemoth of Hebrew lore (Seife, 2000).

Any difficulty on the road to understanding the infinite could, especially in the distant past, look like a threat to the place of God. Some imagined that the infinite might be something even God could not grasp. The list of natural numbers (1, 2, 3, 4…) posed exactly such a challenge. Augustine took the matter with great seriousness and devoted much of his writing to it. In *The Infinite Book* (2005), John D. Barrow quotes Augustine hurling charges of blasphemy at any who maintain that "God's foreknowledge cannot embrace things which are infinite." Numbers are infinite, Augustine reasons, but that does not stop God from knowing every numeral; God's knowledge does not reach some highest sum and halt. To God, every infinity is somehow made finite.

The threat to God from the infinite resurfaced in the Renaissance, though in subtler form. By then God's primary attributes were Being and Infinity — or, put another way, nothing and everything. If mathematicians could learn to confine infinity to a simple sheet of paper, that might spell trouble for God. For this reason one ought not identify God with infinity. God is not finite; yet the fact that some portion of God is intelligible does not diminish God's nature (Barrow, 2005).

Philosophers such as Nicholas of Cusa did not see the infinite as a theological dilemma at all. For Nicholas it was a state of reconciliation between opposites, teaching lessons necessary for understanding reality that the senses could never supply; he openly used mathematics to solve the riddles of theology. Observing finite lines, for instance, one sees a clear difference between a straight line and a curved one; but picture a circle of infinite diameter, and any arc of it becomes indistinguishable from a limitless straight line (Barrow, 2005). The image suggests one in which infinity is indistinguishable from zero — for infinity, on the whole, is undefinable.

In the seventeenth century Blaise Pascal, the French philosopher and mathematician, brought a singular angle to the theology of the infinite, observing that any finite quantity multiplied by infinity yields a limitless one. Pascal wished to persuade atheists that belief in God was the "best bet." God either exists or does not, but reason alone cannot decide, since an infinite gulf separates humanity from God. The best wager, Pascal argued, is belief: the security gained is unlimited if God exists, while the loss if God does not is merely the finite time spent in faith. For the atheist the calculus is reversed — the loss is infinite if God exists, and the gain merely finite if God does not (Barrow, 2005).

Pascal also confronted the problem of double infinity. Infinity, he held, is met with daily in the world even though human beings cannot conceive it. One of the two infinities is a potential infinity, by which numbers increase without limit and never reach an end; the other is the infinite smallness present in all things, by which anything may be divided forever. Motion, space, and time, he reasoned accordingly, could each be broken indefinitely into an endless succession of stages (Barrow, 2005). These arguments no longer hold. If the universe is finite — as modern cosmology suggests it may well be — the case for an ever-expanding nature fails. There is also a maximum speed for all motion that carries information: the speed of light in a perfect vacuum. And space and time, finally, are not infinitely divisible.

René Descartes, the natural philosopher and metaphysician, followed Pascal in contemplating the world's double infinity, but he introduced a new notion: increase without limit as the *indefinite*. He avoided the word "infinite" so as not to be drawn into disputes about potential infinities. As Barrow (2005) puts it, "Descartes wants to dispatch all other discussion about actual infinities to the dustbin of unwarranted speculation. Our human limitations make it impossible to discuss the subtleties of the infinite." Descartes examined examples that would later prove central to a theory of actual mathematical infinities, all while insisting that we must not dwell on them, since human beings are finite and it is absurd to try to grasp the infinite. The limitless, he reasoned, is for God alone.

Immanuel Kant, one of the most influential figures in Western philosophy, held that the universe was infinite — in size and in the infinitude of possibilities unfolding within it — and that in this respect the cosmos intimated God. Yet beliefs about such things are not equal to knowledge. Kant placed the infinities among the realities while denying that human perception, bounded by the senses, could ever reach them. In his view actual infinities do not exist; there can be only finite perceptions, or phenomena. His philosophy resembled Nicholas's in treating perceived reality as distinct from reality as it actually is. He too believed the universe infinite in spatial extension and variety — attributes that reflect the character of God, yet make God unimaginable and unpresentable in any limited form (Barrow, 2005).

Strangely, zero can be infinity and infinity can be zero. Infinity is just a point on a sphere like any other, as the mathematician Georg Friedrich Bernhard Riemann pointed out; he analyzed and classified the points at which a function "blows up," calling them singularities. As Seife (2000) defines it, the infinite is "something that can stay the same size even when you subtract from it." Nothing can be added to or taken from infinity — a fact that considerably weakens the position on infinity held by Paul Copan and William Lane Craig (2004). The mathematician Georg Cantor, for his part, held that there is an infinite number of infinities nested within one another, and that at the summit stands an infinity that engulfs all the rest: God, the infinity that surpasses all understanding.

Copan and Craig (2004) explain that creating the cosmos out of nothing does not mean producing it out of some stuff that is "nothing," but out of nothing at all. Nothing whatsoever, apart from God's sovereign Word, gave the universe its being. Creation is neither in God, nor part of God, nor made out of God; there is no blurring of the line between creature and Creator. As they understand it, the created universe is utterly other than God. Out of the qualitative infinitude of God, the finite and temporal state of reality came to be. Creation out of nothing therefore entails that the universe depends on God for its very persistence.

Oneness resembles "none-ness," because merger signifies dissolution: nothing is the one thing, with no other to which it might relate itself. In some respects the distinction is trivial, but in others perhaps not. Dismantling the otherness of God can take a sharp scriptural turn, for there is only "one God and Father of all, who is over all and through all and in all" (Ephesians 4:6). That God is *in* all seems to indicate God's oneness with all — or at least that the position of God as wholly "other" is untenable. After all, the Bible declares that in God "we live and move and have our being" (Acts 17:28). God, then, is not wholly distinct from creation. Like most theology, Copan and Craig's position is a doctrine of humanity; that, however, is not the present concern. The point is that if humans and God are one, as the Bible guarantees (John 17:21), then in a nonlocalized state God is the very quantum soup from which all things arise.

The concepts of zero and infinity, nothing and everything, have always looked curiously alike. Multiplying anything by zero yields zero; multiplying anything by infinity yields infinity. Dividing anything by zero produces infinity, and dividing anything by infinity produces zero. Add zero to a number and the number is unchanged; add a number to infinity and the infinite is unchanged. Zero and infinity are two poles of the same reality — yin and yang, equal in power within the realm of numbers. To understand the infinite, Seife (2000) writes, one must study zero.

### Ontological Mathematics

A transition from 0 to 1 is impossible in higher mathematics. A number is "regular" if it cannot be reached by adding together fewer numbers than itself, each smaller than itself. It is easy to see that 1 is regular: one cannot reach it from below, since all there is to work with is 0, and the sum of any quantity of 0's is 0 and nothing more. By this route, going from nothing to something is not feasible. Oddly, 1 is not the only number with this property. The number 2 is regular too, since one cannot reach it by adding fewer than two numbers below it; moving from unity to plurality is thus an impossibility. The remaining finite numbers lack the property and can be reached from below. The first infinite number, however — represented by the Greek letter omega — is necessarily regular: one cannot reach it by totaling any finite assemblage of finite numbers. So one cannot get from the finite limit to infinity (Holt, 2012).

The simple equation 0 = 1 − 1 captures the fact that 1 and −1 sum to 0. One can also imagine the inverse: not only do 1 and −1 couple to make 0, but 0 may peel apart into 1 and −1. These are opposites of a kind — like positive and negative energy, like the matter and antimatter of quantum mechanics, like the yin and yang of Chinese philosophy. More telling still, −1 is equivalent to 1 run backward in time. Opposites are individuated by their direction of travel through time; in time's absence, −1 and 1 cancel and converge in 0. It seems paradoxical — though it may not be — that time allows the two opposites to peel apart, and that this very peeling specifies the inception of time. The impromptu construction of the universe was inaugurated in just this way (Holt, 2012).

Set theory is introduced to children early in their schooling, where they first meet the empty set — the set with no members, such as the set of months that have 32 days. It is commonly written as {} (brackets containing nothing) or by the symbol Ø. Children often ask how it can be a collection if it contains nothing. Many great mathematicians, Richard Dedekind and Ernst Zermelo among them, dismissed the empty set as improper and worthless. A more metaphysically liberal stance, however, grants that empty sets exist. Once that is admitted, an unending hierarchy of subsets emerges: Ø, then the set containing it, {Ø}, then the set containing both, {Ø, {Ø}}, then the set containing that one in turn — and so on, *ad infinitum* (Holt, 2012).

Ontological mathematics concerns the numerical nature of reality, and zero is among its central topics. Mike Hockney, in *Ontological Mathematics* (2015), writes: "Zero is not a normal number. It's a resultant number, a net number that arises wherever we find perfect positive/negative balance. Ontologically, this occurs outside space and time in the frequency domain of the mind/soul." Hockney argues that, within Illuminism, Fourier mathematics is what creates matter — through the conversion of Fourier frequency functions into Fourier spacetime functions. On his account, everything comes from a monadic mathematical nothing.

Equivalently, ontological mathematics is an expression of the Principle of Sufficient Reason (PSR), according to which everything must have a reason, cause, or ground. Mathematics is something, yet its sum is nothing. The sublime symmetry between positive and negative numbers requires that everything reduce to nothing through the relation between real and imaginary numbers. Wherever one finds a reference to nothing, one finds the trace of two somethings that have exactly balanced it. By itself, nothing is impossible; and just as surely, something unbalanced by nil is impossible too. Nothing must always be accompanied by something, as symmetry — the very quintessence of the PSR — demands (Hockney, 2015).

It follows that, logically, nothing at all might have come to be. This "null world" is an ontologically possible reality, though here the word *world* departs from ordinary usage. As Holt (2012) writes, "Unlike other possible worlds, it involves no spacetime, no container or stage or arena of any sort. When we talk about 'it' we're … merely talking about one of the various ways reality might have turned out …" The formula (x) ~ (x = x) captures the idea. Yet though the formula points toward the null world, it does not belong to it; for, as Holt avers, one can say nothing of nothing. Mathematics merely furnishes a way to refer to nullity in descriptive terms. Logical consistency is the null world's outstanding quality — a true virtue indeed.

In ontological geometry there is no need to choose flat space over curved, or curved over flat. An absolute geometry must describe reality without bias, embracing both the Big Bang and Leibniz's monadology of dimensionless monadic minds; it must, therefore, contain dimensionless points at its core. That site must be the first and last aspect of a dynamic universe. As Hockney (2015) avows, the "geometry of reality must accommodate zero, infinity, and all numbers in between." Absolute geometry should accommodate Newton's flat space and the curved spacetime of the German-born physicist Albert Einstein alike — the point at infinity figuring as a plane that supplies flatness, and as a sphere that supplies curvature. Hockney is keen to stress that space and time do not exist in any ultimate sense, as classical physics would have it; they are mathematical constructs emanated from that which is most real — the monads of Leibniz and the God Equation.

Monads are flawless, dimensionless zeros, and there is an infinite number of them absent any sufficient reason for a limit. Were a highest number ever reached, all of reality would submit to it, and the total of every monad would equal that number; the sum would be vast but not infinite. Since infinities do exist, each number must merge with ontologically calculable causal chains, lest the fabric of reality be rent asunder and the universe destroyed. Infinities are possible abstract restrictions tied to phase changes and event horizons. The finite world does not perceive infinities funneled through one-way valves charted to a finite limit; only at a certain number does one reach the infinity associated with a singularity (Hockney, 2015).

Mathematicians and scientists alike labor over the definition of mathematics itself. What is mathematics? Does it have an ontological basis? Is it a language, a construct of the mind, a byproduct of the mind's configuration? Why does it drive science? If science is real, must mathematics be real too? And if mathematics is unreal, why does it describe nature so well? Hockney's answer is that "Mathematics … is ontological" (Hockney, 2015). It has both a Form and a content. The Form is its rational aspect, governed by rules that people mistakenly regard as imaginary and merely conceptual. But mathematics also has content — even if that content does not itself appear mathematical.

Ontological mathematics, as Hockney sees it, is about rationalism, Form, and the sinusoidal waves that carry information. The human mind, however, attunes not to those waves — the abstract mathematical principles themselves — but to the content they transmit. Mathematical content reaches the senses as colors, emotions, smells, and tastes, and is never experienced as numbers, even though without its mathematical basis none of these could exist. Mathematics is nature's language, and Hockney holds that there can be no competing natural languages, since they would fail to agree with one another (Hockney, 2015).

Since nothing can prevent nothing's existence, nothing must exist necessarily. Hockney speaks of a "full nothing," which he describes as the very notion of being (Hockney, 2015). The reality of nothing is discoverable within the idea of it: to conceive that it exists is to make it exist. If one can perceive the essence of nothing, then an actual nothing — which Hockney calls a monad — exists; and if one monad exists, an infinite number do, absent any sufficient reason for a limit. The monad is the source of all mathematical perfections. Existence is itself a mathematical perfection, and therefore belongs to the nature of a mathematical reality: the monad.

Things are real insofar as they are ontologically rational. Something is logical if it is compatible with the PSR and never contradicts it, and whatever is noncontradictory is purely mathematical. Hockney defines existence as "the rational possibility of existence combined with the absence of anything to prevent that possibility from being actualized" (Hockney, 2015). A thing is necessary if it is rationally possible and not logically preventable; indeed, whatever is not expressly forbidden is obligatory. Since something exists, it exists because nothing has obstructed its mathematical potential.

### A First Temporal Instant

In *Creation out of Nothing* (2004), Copan and Craig follow in the footsteps of John Philoponus, the Alexandrian Aristotelian commentator and the last champion of creation *ex nihilo* before the coming of Islam. In *Against Aristotle* and *On the Eternity of the World*, Philoponus opened the line of inquiry that argued for creation *ex nihilo* on the grounds that an infinite past is impossible. Medieval Muslim and Jewish theologians built on this tradition and carried it back into Christian scholastic thought.

Copan and Craig (2004) cite the Sunni mystic al-Ghazali, who in *al-Iqtisad fi'l-i'tiqad* writes of the Creator's creation: "Every being which begins has a cause for its beginning; now the world is a being which begins; therefore, it possesses a cause for its beginning." One might ask whether every being that begins really must have a cause, and whether it is wise to call the world "a being which begins" without sufficient evidence. Even so, Copan and Craig hold to the doctrine of creation *ex nihilo*, resting on the notion of a temporal past that had a beginning.

According to Copan and Craig (2004), "something cannot come into being uncaused out of nothing." Yet, as Hockney (2015) has shown, either something does come into being from nothing or something *is* nothing. Copan and Craig define the beginning as "a first standard event," and ask whether there was a first temporal instant — a primeval event occupying the nonzero temporal interval, absolutely first, preceded by nothing. If the universe is eternal, there is no first; and if creation proceeds *ad infinitum*, no first temporal event occurred either. None of these possibilities has been proven.

To support their denial of infinity, Copan and Craig (2004) take up the famous example of Hilbert's Hotel, found in *One, Two, Three… Infinity* by the physicist George Gamow. Imagine first a hotel with finitely many rooms: when a would-be guest arrives, he is turned away, for every room is taken. Now imagine the hotel has infinite vacancies: when a new guest arrives, he is admitted, and every current occupant simply moves one room to the right, on into infinity. Because an infinity once full is now able to absorb more, Copan and Craig conclude that an actual infinity cannot exist, and they pursue this line at length. But as already noted, infinity is a number that can be neither increased nor reduced.

As Victor J. Stenger notes in *God and the Multiverse* (2014), Copan and Craig held that a universe without a creator would have begun an infinite time ago, so that no one could ever have reached the present. Philoponus made the same argument in the sixth century, as did the ninth-century Arabic scholar Ya'qūb ibn Isḥāq al-Kindī. Most physicists and cosmologists, and even many mathematicians, use "infinity" to mean something merely endless or boundless — often just a very large number. Cantor demonstrated that infinity is not a real number, and in 1925 the universal mathematician David Hilbert declared that infinity flees reality, existing nowhere in nature and furnishing no basis for rational thought.

Copan and Craig invoke Hilbert to support their case for an origin of the universe: if infinity does not exist, then reality cannot be timeless and could not have begun an infinite time ago, for that would have required an eternity to reach this moment. Yet as Stenger puts it, "an eternal universe would not have had a beginning an infinite time ago. It had no beginning. Running a clock backward and counting the ticks: −1, −2, −3 …, will never get it to −∞" (Stenger, 2014). Another way to see that the universe need not have a beginning, Stenger adds, is that it need not have an end. Cosmology suggests the cosmos will expand forever; time is symmetric and so requires no beginning. Even if one could fix an initial point for the Big Bang, it need not be the origin of everything. If no end will come, then no beginning came either.

On the World Science Festival panel *Pondering the Imponderables* (2018), the South African mathematician George Ellis argued that it is unreasonable to assume an actual infinity exists, given its uncountable nature: "Infinity is beyond what we can ever attain. It doesn't matter how many things you've done, how much stuff you've counted, how far you've gone, you haven't taken the first step on the road to infinity." David Albert, a physicist and philosopher, challenged this, replying that the undetectability of infinity does not mean our current theories give us no reason to think infinities exist. The Schrödinger equation is superb at predicting quantum behavior, and it offers no viable reason for time ever to end. Science may never prove infinity, but theory gives ample reason to believe it may exist.

### The Geometer's Creation

As John Michell and Allan Brown see it, infinity and immortality are not properties of matter (Michell & Brown, 2012). They note that the same body of scientific knowledge yields many conflicting beliefs, even among scientists of equal education and standing. Because views on the origin of the universe are so numerous, Michell and Brown conclude that nothing can be known for certain — and, what is more, never will be. The scientific quest for a single universal diagram of physics is, on their telling, misguided, destined to end only in despair, uncertainty, and skepticism.

The revered polymath Galileo Galilei, as Michell and Brown quote him, held that the grand book of the universe "was written in the language of mathematics, and its characters are triangles, circles, and other geometrical figures, without which it is impossible to understand a single word of it" (Michell & Brown, 2012). This is the customary method of cosmology, practiced by the schools of antiquity such as the Pythagoreans and defended by Galileo in the seventeenth century. Traditional cosmology depicts the unfolding of a central point out of the patterns of geometry — often pictured as a compass drawing a circle — and that point represents the pole of the universe. In the common theme it has zero dimensions, like the monadic minds of infinite zeroes: zero in some respects, one in others, the difference slight. Where the center is the zero-pole, or the one, the circle is the universe — the revolving disc, the sphere — and it must have a central axis upon which to spin.

This fixed point goes by many names: Allah, the First Cause, God, the Great Spirit, the Higher Power, the Ultimate, and so on. It is an archetype with many names and no descriptions. The Creator is the universal pillar at the center, the divine law — unchanging, eternal, the harmony to which the universe turns. As Michell and Brown profess, the Creator dreamed the world into existence as an emanation of his or her own ideal state. The universe is perfectly spherical, a living creature flawless in mind, body, and spirit; it is the one autonomous entity that exists, and every other individual is an aspect of it. Even should there be other dimensions and modes of existence, they all compose a single Whole. The universe, therefore, is the One; and since it is self-sustaining, outside the One there is nothing — zero (Michell & Brown, 2012).

The traditional creation myth can be rendered in geometrical terms, which are universal principles of nature. Geometry is as deeply entrenched in the natural world as any of its products, if not more so — ever present and dispersed throughout the whole universe. It may even be said to predate the cosmos, given that the cosmos is not eternal; and in any case, all mathematical principles are dimensionless, and so serve as the causal gears of the cosmic process. In the Hebrew tradition, God framed and measured creation. As Michell and Brown (2012) note, the apocryphal Book of Wisdom proclaims that God "made all things in number and measure and weight."

In sacred geometry the Creator made everything in a fundamental, practical way, keeping the work simple enough for all to follow. The material part of creation was realized in three phases, using the first three segments of geometry. The first is the ratio of 1 to π, between a circle's diameter and its circumference. The second is the ratio of 1 to the square root of 2 (1:1.4142…), between a square's side and its diagonal. The third is the ratio of 1 to the square root of 3 (1:1.732…), between the length and breadth of a rhombus. There is a fourth ratio as well — the square root of 5 and its derivative, the golden section — which is bound up with the formation of life and appears in the later geometry of the numbers 5 and 10 (Michell & Brown, 2012).

### Null Cosmology

The original act of creation works much like a pop-up book. The book lies flat like any other, but when opened, houses, animals, landscapes, and moving parts spring up. So too there is an initial flatness to the quantum substrate of reality, and then there are objects in spacetime — and everything comes to be at once. The behavior of lone particles does not give an accurate picture of reality. For a large material body to exist, atoms, molecules, and subatomic particles need not assemble like the parts of a house; rather, the subatomic particles carry space and time along with them (Chopra & Kafatos, 2017).

It is possible that the laws of physics existed before the cosmos, like the transcendental, eternal Forms of Plato. Even so, they would still be only mathematical patterns and sets of equations; they lack ontic clout, and some demiurge would seem to be needed to move them to action. As Hawking asks in *A Brief History of Time*, quoted by Holt, "What is it that breathes fire into the equations and makes a universe for them to describe? … Why does the universe go to all the bother of existing?" (Holt, 1994). If the laws of physics came into being at the same moment as the universe, they cannot describe it; and if they existed before the universe, then nothing can account for their being, let alone explain why the null world should be bound to obey them.

Terence Witt envisions the cosmos as frozen for eternity, unchanging from moment to moment (Witt, 2007). The universe had no origin, will have no end, and never changes — a condition Witt calls temporal equivalence. Moreover, the mass of the cosmos and of each of its constituents never varies, since the total distributed energy stays constant down to the relative position of the last electron; this Witt terms ultrastasis, the most exhaustive expression of temporal equivalence. Moments in time are unquantifiable because the differences between them are unreal: at any one moment the universe may be said to be the same as at any other. Every instant in the universe's history is therefore located somewhere in space right now.

The concept of time is slippery, yet inseparable from the way reality is experienced. Chopra and Kafatos (2017) distinguish two forms of time: time as we know it, and a strange pre-time, or no-time. Einstein made his peace with time by declaring the past and future illusions, holding that only the present exists. The physicist Erwin Schrödinger drew nearer to this mystical view as his grasp of the quantum world deepened. Along the same lines, where spiritual teachers speak of the infinite nature of God's time, cosmologists now say the same of the multiverse. Eternity does not mean an extremely long time; it is timelessness. The moment of creation — whether by God, the Big Bang, or any cosmological model one prefers — took no time at all. The pure chaos of the Planck era preceded time, which was but one ingredient among many in the quantum soup.

In all likelihood, time is already eternal. It shows two faces — one of personal experience, the other of the outer world of empirical observation — and the two intertwine. Time seems to drag when unenjoyed and to fly when enjoyed. One may grant primacy to objective clock time or let subjective personal time prevail; in intensely personal moments the human element shows itself more plainly than usual, since ordinarily we take it for granted. As Chopra and Kafatos write, "Time doesn't 'flow' in the quantum field, where all of reality exists as pure potential. The quantum field is outside our commonsense notion of time, and when a particle emerges from the field, it has no history. Particles are tied to an on/off switch, not to the past" (Chopra & Kafatos, 2017). It follows, just as Einstein held, that time exists only now, in real time, and that the flow of time — encompassing all universal processes — is an illusion.

In contrast to Copan and Craig (2004), Witt hypothesizes that there are no limits to energy, space, or time — his is the idea of a real, infinite universe (Witt, 2007). Copan and Craig wished to show that the cosmos could not be infinite and therefore required a creator. Witt counters that if one boarded a spaceship, chose a direction, and traveled forever, one would keep finding new objects in uncharted territory. Any boundary on a measurable feature of reality is, for Witt, a limit imposed on the state of nonexistence; and nonexistence is free of diameter and mass, those finite qualities characteristic of existence. Unboundedness is thus an apt descriptor for nothingness — the reverse of how traditional mathematics portrays it.

The universe is the wellspring of all data and knowledge. According to Witt, "The only meaningful generalizations within this context are not human creations; they are direct reflections of reality's underlying structure" (Witt, 2007). To reach an accurate definition of infinity, Witt holds, one must look to the properties of the universe rather than to the assertions of mathematics. The relation between limited and limitless numbers is one of scale, not of substance. The breadth of nullity is an infinite quantity of meters, and no meter can be removed from space without leaving it incomplete; each meter of the universe's diameter is necessary to define the scope of nothingness. Infinite numbers are built from finite ones, just as the universe's diameter is built from individual meters.

Hawking indirectly disputes some of the precepts of null physics in *The Theory of Everything* (2002). He notes that galaxies appear to be receding from the Earth rather than holding still, which points to a moment of origin and to expansion; the light from distant galaxies is red-shifted, diminished by the Doppler effect, and this serves as evidence. It is curious that Newton and others did not discover this earlier, for a static universe would soon begin to contract under gravity. The expansion was recognizable from Newton's time onward, yet the seventeenth, eighteenth, and nineteenth centuries passed without the breakthrough. Even Einstein, having built the general theory of relativity in 1915, remained convinced that the universe was necessarily static.

According to Hawking (2002), a striking feature of the first kind of Friedmann model — the model describing universal expansion and contraction — is that the universe is not infinite in space, contrary to null physics, and yet has no boundaries either. One need not have an infinite universe for it to be boundless; as Hawking explains, space and time can be infinite without edge or boundary. It would be impossible to travel around the universe and return to one's starting point, for the universe would collapse to zero density before the journey could be completed. More tellingly still, Hawking too sees the universe as exhibiting the principle of zero: "Now, twice zero is also zero. Thus, the universe can double the amount of positive matter energy and also double the negative gravitational energy … When the universe doubles in size, the positive matter energy and the negative gravitational energy both double, so the total energy remains zero" (Hawking, 2002).

There is, however, a limit to science where the era before the Big Bang is concerned — granting that there was a Big Bang at all. The reason is that at the singularity from which the universe would have erupted, the laws of physics break down (Hawking, 2002). Stenger, in *God and the Multiverse* (2014), notes that current physical knowledge reaches back only to 10⁻¹² seconds after the Big Bang; beyond that point only speculation remains, the province of philosophy and religion. Stenger assures his readers that he posits no ultimate, metaphysical reality — yet in affirming that precise knowledge of any such reality is unattainable, he confirms the very ineffable actuality of which the metaphysicians, mystics, and philosophers speak.

Stenger reports that in 1970 Hawking and his colleague Roger Penrose, the English mathematical physicist, showed that within the framework of general relativity the universe began as a minute point of mass-energy called a singularity (Stenger, 2014). In his 1988 book *A Brief History of Time*, however, Hawking argued that no singularity stood at the origin of the universe, and that quantum theory allows the notion to be dispensed with. The point of origin on a spacetime diagram requires a finite amount of space and time, not an infinitesimal point. Looking still to the unimaginably small, Stenger writes that such a region must be a four-dimensional sphere whose spatial radius equals the Planck length and whose temporal dimension is intrinsic to Planck time — small, but not a singular, infinitesimal point.

Hawking and other cosmologists theorize that this universe's spacetime is finite and unbounded, like the surface of a higher-dimensional basketball. Such a spacetime, together with all its contents, is not hard to imagine away: as the basketball deflates, its radius shrinks toward zero, until spacetime disappears and leaves total nothingness behind. A scientific definition of nothing, then, is "a closed spherical spacetime of zero radius." On this view the container argument fails, whatever its form: if spacetime is not a genuine object but only a category of relations among things, it vanishes along with them and poses no obstacle to the possibility of nothingness (Holt, 2012).

The universe need have neither beginning nor end. The physicist and cosmologist Alexander Vilenkin proposed the idea of eternal inflation in 1983, on which the universe, once begun, never stops expanding, with new macrocosms generated continually. The Russian-American physicist Andrei Linde extended the notion in 1986, showing that the cosmos might replicate without limit and so might have no beginning or end at all. Eternal inflation may yield universes within universes, fractal-like in composition: as one bubble universe expands toward indeterminate proportions, other bubbles germinate in the empty region around it, and the process continues unabated, forever (Stenger, 2014).

### The Quantum Zero

Around the time mathematicians were uncovering the link between zero and infinity, physicists began to see zero appear in the observable world; zero passed, in effect, from mathematics into physics. In thermodynamics it became an unbridgeable barrier — the coldest possible temperature. In Einstein's general relativity it became a black hole, a vast star that devours whole suns. In the quantum world it became an extraordinary wellspring of energy, infinite and omnipresent, found in even the most cavernous vacuum — a phantom agency governed by absolutely nothing (Seife, 2000).

For science, the discovery of absolute zero meant something quite different from Newton's laws. Newton's equations had given physicists the power to predict the courses of planets and the motions of objects with great accuracy. The discovery of absolute zero by the Scots-Irish physicist William Thomson — better known as Lord Kelvin — by contrast, told physicists what they could *not* do: they could never reach absolute zero. They were disappointed at first, yet the discovery heralded a new branch of physics, thermodynamics, which Seife describes as the study of how heat and energy behave. Thermodynamics erected immovable barriers that no scientist could cross, however valiantly they tried (Seife, 2000).

Kelvin noticed something peculiar in the law of the inventor and balloonist Jacques-Alexandre Charles, which concerns the relationship between the volume of a gas and its temperature; that peculiarity, Seife writes, is "the scepter of zero" (Seife, 2000). Charles, fascinated by the properties of gases, observed that as one lowers the temperature and volume of a balloon, it grows smaller and smaller until, in theory, it would take up no space at all. Charles's law thus implies that a gas-filled balloon must recede toward zero volume. What this really means is that the balloon reaches its smallest possible volume, not that it occupies no space; when it can shrink no further and the temperature can fall no lower, this is absolute zero. Temperature is measured universally on Kelvin's scale: where the centigrade scale sets zero at the freezing point of water, the Kelvin scale sets zero at total zero.

Aristotle held that emptying reality in any empirical sense was impossible, and he produced a range of scientific and conceptual arguments to show that space could never be completely voided. The Aristotelian doctrine that nature abhors a vacuum endured until the mid-seventeenth century, when it was contested and overthrown by the physicist Evangelista Torricelli, one of Galileo's students. Torricelli built the first barometer and showed that nature's supposed abhorrence of a vacuum was nothing but atmospheric pressure bearing down on objects. Even so, this was not true nothingness. Contemporary science teaches that even the perfect void contains something, and in physics that something is quantified energy; technically, space is as void as it can be only when it holds no energy (Holt, 2012).

If one tries to produce a vacuum state by reducing a region of space to its lowest possible energy, a curious thing happens: the Higgs field surfaces of its own accord. This field cannot be banished, for it is in fact a negative contribution to the energy of the emptied space. Astonishingly, the Higgs field is a something that holds less energy than nothing, and it brings with it a host of virtual particles that ceaselessly pop into and out of existence. As it turns out, the space inside a vacuum is bustling with activity (Holt, 2012).

Chopra and Kafatos write that the pre-created state may be dimensionless — mathematically occupying zero dimensions — and that every particle now emerging issues from a domain of zero dimensions, the quantum vacuum (Chopra & Kafatos, 2017). Some physicists reason that only two numbers exist: zero and infinity. At zero, the trick of something-from-nothing occurs; infinity names the number of possibilities that can potentially occur. Just as with zero and infinity, in the Planck era there was no time, no before or after, and everywhere and nowhere lay in the same place.

Einstein, who scarcely looked like a revolutionary at the time, wrote a paper in 1905 that would later win him the Nobel Prize. It explained the photoelectric effect and helped raise the profile of quantum mechanics, whose establishment would in turn make room for the puzzling powers of zero. Light, Einstein proposed, comes in tiny packets of energy called photons. As physicists unraveled the complexities of quantum mechanics, strange quantities like zeroes began to appear in their equations, betraying the presence of the elusive zero-point energy. In the 1920s the German physicist Werner Heisenberg observed that the equations of the quantum universe carried a surprising consequence: uncertainty. Nothing is a force precipitated by Heisenberg's uncertainty principle (Seife, 2000).

Classical and quantum field theories share the mathematical properties of one-dimensional simple harmonic oscillators — rudimentary pendulums, for instance. Here Ø is a field value related to the displacement of a counterweight from its symmetrical position. Because of the uncertainty principle, the quantum harmonic oscillator is never at rest; it oscillates about its balance point with a minimal energy known as the zero-point energy. A quantum fluctuation is any variance of Ø (Stenger, 2014).

Zero pours infinite energy into the quantum vacuum, and in relativity it gives rise to the paradox of the infinite and of zeroes alike, in the form of a black hole. A black hole forms when an immense star buckles and vanishes. Physicists know of no force in the universe that can halt its collapse, so indomitable is its gravitational pull. It is ironic that a black hole is nothing and yet that nothing can stop it: nothing can stop nothing. As a dying star grows ever smaller, it finally becomes zero and crams itself into zero space. So paradoxical is this entity that some scientists speculate it might be used to travel faster than light and backward through time (Seife, 2000).

At the so-called fringe of physics are those who believe zero-point energy could be the ultimate fuel — and here some in the mainstream draw a line. Both quantum mechanics and general relativity suggest that the power of zero is infinite, so it is no surprise that people would wish to tap its abundance. Some warn that extracting energy from a vacuum is dangerous, and that the vacuums physicists observe may be false vacuums; in their view, prying too far could tear apart every atom in the universe. As Seife reassures, however, this scenario is highly unlikely (Seife, 2000). In any case, tapping the source may prove impossible, or, if possible, slow in coming — so one should be patient and watchful.

### Absolutely Nothing

In *Our Undiscovered Universe* (2007), Witt argues that everything is rooted in nothing. In trying to solve what he calls the Prime Paradox, he sets out five stages by which existence derives from nothingness. First, nothingness, the supreme reality, presides over all: nothing — no stars, no planets, no space, no time — can be said to exist; all is void. Second, existence opposes nonexistence and is markedly other than it. Third, given the completeness of nothingness, the only thing other than it must be a subset of it, which Witt calls incomplete nonexistence; nothingness, being composed of nothing, requires a subsidiary composition, an incomplete version of nil. Fourth, since only two states exist — existence and nonexistence — nothing must be either complete or incomplete. Fifth, incomplete nonexistence amounts to existence, and so authenticates it.

Nothing, then, can have only a component of itself. On the left side of a null set, zero or nothingness prevails; on the right side subsists a relationship between null sets. Once there was only nonexistence; now there is a correlation among nonexisting sets, and these sets compose existence. As Witt puts it, "The only component nonexistence can have is nonexistence, as it is a total void" (Witt, 2007). Nonexistence nonetheless retains a component sufficient for a relationship — namely, a relationship with existence in its untainted form. Everything, on Witt's account, is composed of the same thing: nothing.

In "Nothing ventured" (1994), Holt calls the cosmos "a mere interlude between two nothings." He recounts the famous God equations of William Temple, a bishop of the Church of England. Temple proposed that God minus the world equals God, while the world minus God equals nothing. His arithmetic ran into trouble, however, when manipulating these equations produced a startling result: God minus God equals God — which is to say that God equals nothing.

Nothing often means almost nothing — a singularity, or some minuscule region. Even if it is little, it is still something, and so null theorists invariably need a kind of orphic seed or mathematical fuzz to set the cosmological process going. As Holt shows, the American scientist Ed Tryon's universe emerges from a false vacuum — a tiny patch of empty spacetime effervescing with particles and fields (Holt, 1994). Hawking imagines that the cosmos might have arisen from a three-dimensional configuration of zero volume, close to nil but no cigar. Others invoke a primeval dust of dimensionless, structureless points and rudimentary geometries — a sort of quantum *tohu-bohu* chaos, kin to the ruminations of the ancients. These are all faint intimations of nothing, not nullity itself; one could hold them in hand, hardly the stuff of existential dread.

Alexander Vilenkin, a Ukrainian émigré cosmologist and "nothing" theorist, comes closest to a true creation *ex nihilo*. He says the universe arose from nothing, and he means it precisely: his nothing lacks matter, space, and time. By a quantum procedure called tunneling — which permits the breaching of otherwise impassable barriers — spacetime materialized out of nothing into a host of prospective universes, and thence into actuality. Because this process creates time, the operations themselves are atemporal: the sequence is rational, not temporal, governed by the laws of physics (Holt, 1994).

The transition from nothing to something is undeniably mysterious. At the Planck scale, space and time break down entirely. The original constants of nature were either strikingly different or did not exist at all. In the Planck dimension space and time turn foamy; there are no directions — no up or down, no left or right. Mathematics is the ideal way to speculate about this state, since it seems to remain constant across dimensions. The pre-created state resembles the quantum vacuum. In classical physics the void is thought to be empty; in quantum theory it is not empty at all but full of quantum stuff — overflowing with energy imperceptible in the discernible cosmos (Chopra & Kafatos, 2017).

### The Philosophical Absolute

For Anaximander of Miletus, the Ionian philosopher, the primal element is the Apeiron — the indeterminate, the unlimited, the infinite. It is not water or any other ingredient, but something altogether distinct and without natural limits. The Apeiron is beyond definition: immortal, imperishable, indestructible, and uncaused, yet the cause of all things. In Anaximander's cosmology, generation goes hand in hand with destruction. He envisions the origin of the universe as a process that separates the hot from the cold: fire lies at the world's periphery, glimpsed through the holes we call stars, while the cold, wet earth sits at the center. Anaximander anticipated the modern theory of evolution, proposing that animals first emerged from water or sun-warmed mud, later moved onto land, and that humans descended from fish (Lamúa, 2013).

Pythagoras of Samos, Aristotle, and Claudius Ptolemy are jointly responsible for the Greek model of the universe — and for its conspicuous lack of zero. Their cosmos leaves "zero room" for nothing, and for this reason the West failed to adopt zero for almost two millennia. The omission impeded the development of mathematics and science and produced a chaotic calendar. To advance, the West would first have to dismantle its model of the universe, a process that began with Pythagorean number-shapes such as the pentagram — a glimpse of the infinite that preceded the acceptance of zero. Stars, or pentagons, contain ever-smaller pentagons *ad infinitum*; and concealed within the pentagram were the lines that became the supreme symbol of the Pythagoreans, the golden ratio. Pythagoras was fond of music as well, in which the harmony of the monochord mirrored mathematical harmony — the symphony of the universe (Seife, 2000).

Master geometers though they were, the Pythagoreans forbade zero as a possibility; after all, zero could have no shape. Sacred geometry did point toward the unity of being — the "One" — since all points are invisibly connected within symmetrical figures. To accept zero, the Greeks would have had to remake their entire model of mathematics, and this they were unwilling to do. Pythagoras felt threatened by the so-called irrational numbers and took extreme measures to forbid all talk of them. In the end, the cause of the Greek rejection of zero was philosophy itself, not ignorance or the number-shape system. Zero menaced Western doctrine with two venomous notions that would eventually destroy Aristotelian philosophy: the void and the infinite (Seife, 2000).

The doctrine of Heraclitus of Ephesus pointed toward a primal energy — one, or none — that, though divided into opposites, may yet be conceived as working in harmony with all. As Sanford L. Drob observes, Heraclitus anticipated Georg Wilhelm Friedrich Hegel, the German idealist, in linking the opposites with God or the Absolute. Drob quotes Heraclitus: "God is day and night, winter and summer, war and peace, surfeit and hunger; but he takes various shapes, just as fire, when it is mingled with spices, is named according to the savor of each" (Drob, 2017). Heraclitus's God is a unified pair of opposites, more like the power of the universe than an anthropomorphized deity. He saw unity not only in opposites but in all things: the One is made of all things and all things of the One, just as zero is in all things and all things are in zero.

Plato investigated what is truly "real." Things are not as they appear, and so the mind must reckon with the gap between appearance and reality. Plato attempts to formulate a criterion of realness by which to assess whatever presents itself as real. In his day, one must remember, words adequate to reality did not yet exist, nor had the concept of reality been firmly fixed. Plato was a pioneer, employing many approaches and formulations to draw nearer to understanding (Demos, 1966).

According to Demos (1966), being is indefinable in Platonic thought, though it has certain traits that serve as its criterion: selfhood, otherness, and relatedness. In Plato's doctrine the domain of opinion lies halfway between the real and the unreal, and the tangible world is a compound of being and not-being. Plato seems to have regarded realness as a predicate of everything: being is an attribute of things, and more than any other universal character, it is a property of the whole spectrum of the real. Because realness is indefinable, Plato's method is negative — he shows that other philosophers, for all their trying, have failed to define it adequately.

One cannot give an exhaustive account of Plato's position on being within a comparative work, but a framework of his key ideas can be sketched. To the dualists, Plato posed the following alternatives. Either being is something other than each of the two principles they posit, in which case the two become three and fail to deliver a binary vision of being; or being is identical with one of the two principles, in which case the world collapses into uniformity; or, lastly, being appears binary but in fact forms a unity. As Demos maintains, "a dualistic doctrine is either driven to the view that being is over and above the two principles — a category in which they participate; in which case, being has remained indefinable; or it is reduced to some form of monism" (Demos, 1966).

Plato confronted the monists in turn — those who held that all being is one. If all is one, then there are already two things: being and oneness. Hence there are two names, each with its own meaning; and it is absurd, Plato stresses, to claim that two names exist when the assertion is that there is nothing but unity. There is, moreover, a particular difficulty in naming being at all. To give being a name contradicts the monistic stance: either the name is not the object, in which case there are two things, or the name is identical with the object. Two alternatives remain — the name names nothing, or it names itself, making it merely the name of a name (Demos, 1966).

Plotinus is regarded as the founder of Neoplatonism, whose ideology would later exert a heavy influence on Jewish and Christian theology. In Plotinus's *Enneads*, the All, or the One, is composed of contraries; the One, he asserts, is everywhere and nowhere. The cosmos is made of opposites that, viewed together, constitute a single body. Good and evil are real to Plotinus, since a person may act malevolently or benignly. Wickedness has a role to play in the harmony of the universe — but that does not place those who commit evil beyond reproach; for Plotinus, the principle of reason can offer them no excuse (Drob, 2017).

The ancient Greek philosopher Epicurus of Samos took up the atomism of the Magi-instructed astronomer Democritus, with some modifications. As Lamúa (2013) reports, "The two basic principles are 'out of nothing comes nothing' and 'the All must be infinite both in respect of the number of atoms and in respect of the extent of void.'" Reality is atoms and the void, the void being the space in which the quanta move. The quanta are innumerable and the region of space infinite; thus worlds are born and perish, while the universe as a whole is eternal and imperishable. Much as in the uncertainty principle of quantum mechanics, Epicurus grants the atoms total freedom to move spontaneously through the void.

Descartes used mathematics to dismantle the Aristotelian syllogism that had served as a model throughout the Middle Ages. He proposed three substances: thinking substance, the infinite or divine, and extended substance. For the present study the infinite matters most. Descartes did not believe the thinking self to be perfect, but held that it must contain an idea of perfection drawn from elsewhere. As Lamúa (2013) notes, Descartes reasoned: "Knowledge of the idea of perfection, beyond my own imperfection, cannot come from myself, because I am imperfect and what I see is imperfect; rather it must come from a being that is more perfect than I, the creator of this innate idea." The infinite is therefore an uncreated substance, the cause of all created beings — and, as already noted, beyond human comprehension, the sole domain of God.

Baruch Spinoza, the Jewish-Dutch philosopher, speaks of a reality that is the cause of itself and of all things, and whose essence involves existence. He calls this reality "God," or "nature." It is, in Spinoza's view, an infinite being with infinite attributes, partaking in infinite concretions, or modes. All things are therefore supremely good, essential, eternal, infinite, and necessary. Actuality is complete and total and can have no limits, since the parts of its substance are infinite. God and nature are identical, and all physical objects are characteristics of God under the attribute of extension (Lamúa, 2013).

Voltaire — the French writer also known as François-Marie Arouet — proposed two kinds of infinity: infinite duration and infinite space. He doubted whether human beings could form any precise idea of what infinity is, yet held that it is impossible for infinity not to exist. An infinite duration has already elapsed, he argues, and nothing can begin something; thus the extent of reality is infinite. Voltaire pictures an eternity before him and an eternity behind him: as eternity endures, the tide of time rolls on. Something has always been and must ever be, for the duration of infinity is causally linked and self-perpetuating. He proposes an infinity of space as well, though he notes that while no one dares to call it "nothing," no one knows how to define it either (Lamúa, 2013).

Drob (2017) suggests that Hegel may have been the last well-known philosopher to engage the idea of the Absolute directly — whether the doctrine is cast as mystical, philosophical, scientific, or theological matters little (though Manly P. Hall, philosopher and mystic, seems never to receive the credit he deserves). Oddly enough, the dialectical method Hegel championed helped hasten the Absolute's demise, as it gave way to existentialism, deconstruction, and postmodernism. He used the term *dialectic* to denote the tendency of attitudes, entities, events, and ideas to pass over into their opposites. Though Hegel cannot accurately be called a Kabbalistic philosopher, his philosophy grew out of the Chasidic traditions shaped by the Kabbalah.

Hegel sees a dialectic between knowledge and being, and between being and nothingness. Reality, he holds, is "against" and "other than" knowing, though it contains knowledge as a facet of its essence: being is what consciousness produces from it, while information is merely the substance, or reality, of that consciousness. Hegel does not affirm — as some schools of Eastern philosophy would — that knowledge and being, subject and object, are one; for him that would defeat the very purpose of dialectics. Being, he insists, is codependent with its opposite, nothingness (Drob, 2017).

Hegel further took "nothing" and made "something" of it — or perhaps the reverse. He treats the Absolute as Pure Being, wholly indefinite, devoid of qualities, an utter void. Pure Being, for Hegel, is the same as Pure Nothing; the two are dialectical twins. They coexist, but not always peacefully, and something is needed that supersedes them: Becoming. Becoming is the state of teetering on the edge between fading into nothing and emerging into something. As it turns out, nothing and something look the same. Nothing is nice, simple, and self-begetting (Holt, 1994).

### Religio-Philosophical Perspectives

In ancient philosophy absolute reality is essentially unspeakable, and the same holds for Buddhist philosophy. In Buddhism, emptiness is a concept closely aligned with the quantum view of being. In *The Quantum and the Lotus* (2001), Matthieu Ricard and Trinh Xuan Thuan explore the interlocking concepts shared by Buddhism and quantum mechanics. On emptiness, Ricard says: "When Buddhism states that emptiness is the ultimate nature of things, it means that the things we see around us, the phenomena of our world, lack any autonomous or permanent existence."

Ricard is careful to show that emptiness is not a void in which no phenomenon is present. Buddhism is not nihilistic, nor does it hold to any state of total annihilation; emptiness corresponds in no way to absolute nonexistence. The wise, according to Buddhist thought, abide neither in being nor in nonbeing. Since Buddhists seek to understand reality as fully as human capacity allows, they aim to grasp the existence and nonexistence of the particles of matter, and they hold that modern science has helped to clarify the spiritual path rather than to obstruct it (Ricard & Thuan, 2001).

According to Ricard, it is impossible for "nothing" to become "something" — a debatable claim, depending on how one defines nothing. He argues that nothingness is a concept defined only by its relation to existence, and that it has no reality of its own, since nothingness "cannot be conceived in the absence of existence" (Ricard & Thuan, 2001). Here he may be guilty of the philosopher's fallacy, declaring nothingness unreal simply because he cannot conceive it on its own. Nothingness cannot transform; and if something appears, the potential for it to manifest was already present. Buddhism and quantum mechanics converge on the concept of the vacuum, where both affirm that "something" is present within the "nothing." Buddhism holds that things have no independent reality and can therefore have no beginning; it affirms not an absolute origin of the cosmos, but cycles of formation, continuation, destruction, and nonmanifestation (Ricard & Thuan, 2001).

Madhyamaka, the Middle Way school of Buddhism, regards reality as an interdependence of conscious and unconscious phenomena, with neither consciousness nor unconsciousness existing in absolute terms. Events take place neither in the mind nor outside it; they are neither existent nor nonexistent. To realize the ultimate nature of being is to pass beyond the very concepts of being and nonbeing. The postulation of "nothing" is no rejection of reason; it is the recognition that phenomena are not formulaic concepts, and that there are no categories in which to encase the material or the nonmaterial. Hence the distinction between relative and absolute truth — relative truth being how things appear to an individual, with their seemingly fathomable characteristics, and absolute truth being the recognition that those characteristics have no intrinsic existence, so that the ultimate nature of things lies beyond all conception and abstraction (Ricard & Thuan, 2001).

Human beings lack any terminology adequate to define the Absolute in material terms. Only emptiness can be shown — which is, paradoxically, an absolute fullness. Blankness is the space that holds the totality of existence in potential. Materiality must obey the laws of change, which are the laws of nature: every sun will eventually grow dark and cold, and every atom will manifest and vanish again. Of necessity the phenomenal world arises, ages, and decays — entropy prevails in the perceptible spheres. Eastern traditions classify this diversity as Maya, illusion; and so entropy is an unreal phase of nonexistent temporality, masquerading as "all there is" (Hall, 2005).

Yin and yang are central to Chinese philosophy, depicted as the circle for zero — the ground of reality — and as black and white for the opposites, zero and infinity. As the symbol shows, there is a little of nothing in everything and a little of everything in nothing. In Taoism the duality of yin and yang forms a single unified whole; the two principles are not metaphysically distinct, and neither is axiologically superior to the other. Often enough "yin-yang" is spoken as a single word, in one breath, marking the total unification of opposites (Drob, 2017). As Lao Tzu so elegantly puts it in Stephen Mitchell's rendering of the *Tao Te Ching* (2006): "Being and non-being create each other. Difficult and easy support each other. Long and short define each other. High and low depend on each other. Before and after follow each other."

Traditions as diverse as Taoism, Hinduism, and Chabad Chasidism all hold to an integrated worldview in which the conjunction of contraries can spark a restoration of a former unity. This fusion is directly experienced in ecstatic states or mystical union, but never fully spoken in words. Both poles are asserted together as ways of expressing, through inadequate language, the unity of all being. As Drob (2017) puts it, "that God is both identical and non-identical with the world; that the world is both 'many' and 'one'; that God or the Absolute is both the fullness of being and complete emptiness; and that the enlightened individual both retains and surrenders his individuality." In the same way, the Absolute is nothing and everything. Language, by its very nature, categorizes, compartmentalizes, and divides.

The Absolute is coequal with the Ein Sof of the Hebrew Kabbalists. The Jewish mystics used the closed eye to convey the meaning of the blank sheet of paper: the impenetrable "nothing" that reaches the mind through closed eyes imparts the eternal, undefinable, and indiscernible nature of Absolute Being. The Kabbalists called the open eye "the spirit," or "the dot," for with its outward gaze the ego (the "I Am") beholds the limitless array of objects that, like an interlocking web, make up the illusory realm. Turn that same eye inward, however, to contemplate its source, and it meets a bareness that cannot be pierced (Hall, 2005).

Gnosticism likewise examines the coincidence of opposites and the unity of all being. According to the Gnostics, to know the beginning is to know the end. They held that the individual could become one with the knowledge he possessed, and that God and reality were androgynous. Some Gnostics affirmed that God created humanity and that humanity created God in turn. They read the opposing pairs — perfection and imperfection, good and evil — as evidence of the corruption of the creative deity, Yahweh, who failed to make the world in a perfect image (Hall, 2005). Yahweh the demiurge is not the supreme divinity; the Good "should not be confused with a god, since it is greater than a god" (Barnstone & Meyer, 2009).

Jungian psychology regards the World Parents — the collective unconscious — as the origin of reality and the seat from which ego consciousness issues forth. In the beginning, one might say in the time before time, all lay in pristine wholeness. As in other philosophies, this state is indescribable and can only be symbolized. In the creation myths of every people, the creation of light marks the coming of consciousness, and the earliest state is figured by a host of symbols of the first daybreak before light's arrival. Chief among them is the circle, joined by the sphere, the egg, the rotundum, and the alchemical and Platonic round. Ring, round, and orb are all aspects of the Self-contained — timeless and eternal, beyond description yet holding everything in potential. In short: zero and infinity (Neumann, 2014).

### A Mystic's Ineffable Reality

In *Lectures on Ancient Philosophy* (2005), Hall writes: "To define adequately the nature of the Absolute is impossible, for it is everything in its eternal, undivided, and unconditioned state." The ancients, he says, called the Absolute the nothing and the all — in mathematical terms, zero and infinity. No mind can devise an adequate symbol for it; of all symbols, a simple blank sheet of paper may be the most fitting. The blankness of the paper stands for all that cannot be apprehended, visualized, or felt, all that cannot be confined to a cognitive task. It represents eternal, measureless, unlimited space. All things, Hall says, come from it and return to it, yet it neither comes nor goes.

Any figure drawn upon the paper pollutes it. The symbols express conditions abiding upon the face of space, as it were — conditions produced by the substance of space itself. The All is the blank sheet, and anything drawn upon it signifies a fractional phase of it, much as in mathematical set theory. The instant a symbol appears, the paper loses its pristine, limitless blankness. As Hall teaches, the symbols stand for the inventive powers and materials, and the undefiled wholeness of Absolute Being fractures the moment existential components arise. Forms obliterate the formlessness that came before them. Space, in ancient philosophy, is at once the paramount substance and the paramount force, and the sum of both. All that exists is in the void, and everything made emerges within it, for the void is the origin of everything — and yet it is nothing (Hall, 2005).

If Hall and the ancient philosophers are right, then the boundless is the source of everything. Hawking concurs to the extent that the origin of the universe requires some quantity of spacetime, but no singularity and therefore no beginning (Stenger, 2014). This space, Hall says, is not God, nor nature, nor humankind, nor the universe; each is fashioned from emptiness, but only the void is supreme. The void is one with Absolute Spirit and Absolute Matter, so that spirit, matter, and space are one — or none. Space is the positive manifestation of spirit; substance is space manifested negatively. The first Trinity is spirit, matter, and space, answering to the Father, Son, and Holy Ghost. Though pristinely undivided, space separates through a putative division of Absolute Spirit, Absolute Intelligence, and Absolute Matter.

A blank sheet of paper represents eternity; a dot placed upon it represents time. Erase the dot, restore the blank sheet, and time dissolves back into eternity. Ancient philosophy holds two symbols dear: the nothing and the one — the white dot and the paper. As Hall (2005) writes, "Creation traces its origin from the dot — the Primeval Sea, the Egg laid by the White Swan in the field of SPACE." The dot of origin could be a singularity that caused the Big Bang, or the small spatial region examined by Hawking.

Mystics have long struggled to describe their experiences to others, for to say anything about the unfathomable is a difficult, perhaps impossible, task. Such statements must be confined by language, which is in turn limited by its form: logic. As Anna F. Lemkow puts it, "No matter how profound or evocative or affecting, their words are only statements about Reality, not Reality itself" (Lemkow, 1995). One might say that no statement about the ultimate nature of reality can be true, for there is nothing to be said of nothing. Along the path of the evolution of consciousness, every widening and deepening of understanding causes the horizon to recede, so that knowledge is forever boundless and never final.

According to Lemkow (1995), all knowledge is inherently deficient, and even exact science has its peculiar limits. Mathematics, she writes, is inadequate to the inquiry into many kinds of experience — compassion, love, and suffering, for instance. Science cannot account for all of reality, physical reality included, because it cannot separate itself from inward life. She argues further that science gives too little thought to consciousness, even though all knowing, subjective or objective, takes place within. Physics, Lemkow believes, conveys an incomplete picture of the world and fails to tell humanity about what matters most: it does not touch the things most meaningful to human life — beauty, colors, tastes, and sounds. As she tells it, Newton himself eventually abandoned physics for religion, remarking that science was too limited.

## Chapter Five: Discussion

The notions of zero and infinity clearly pose serious difficulties to the human mind. And yet, as the foregoing has shown, these principles dwell within everything known and unknown. The mathematical struggles of early human history — and the subsequent rejection of zero and the void — mark a period of unawareness that eventually gave way to a reconciliation with reality. Viewed in this light, history is humbling: one sees that even the most astute intellects of their respective eras often fell short of complete correctness. People today are no different, and that humility ought to be preserved as the search for knowledge goes on.

The concept of "nothing" is relevant to the metaphysical community and to truth-seekers of every kind. Throughout history, nothing has been mistaken for a "something," as the existentialists concluded; others denied it outright simply because they could not imagine it, mistaking a personal limitation for a constraint upon reality. This dissertation holds that when a metaphysician speaks of nothing, what is expressed is the presence of an abstraction that cannot be pinned down or particularized — not a state of annihilation. Infinity, conversely, has been a point of contention in religious and philosophical circles, embraced by some and reviled by others.

It is difficult to say whether concepts like zero and infinity are the sole province of God — or of the Self, the Ultimate, the All — and equally difficult to know whether they exist in any actual sense. Science teaches, for example, that there is always "something" in the "nothing," while mathematics reveals that infinity is a potential no one can ever fully attain. The two concepts are plainly not only complementary but, in some respects, identical; and in their very likeness they may give way to dissolution. If infinity grows so vast that the number finally becomes a singularity, it is almost nothing — indefinable. Likewise, if space, once wholly emptied, is found to be filled with infinite potential, it is practically "something."

Nothingness does not signify total obliteration, any more than the Buddhist concept of emptiness signifies annihilation. Indeed, nothing is everything and emptiness is full; the vacuum is void only of ego consciousness, while brimming with all else. Scientifically speaking, any adequate "Theory of Everything" will at the same time have to be a "Theory of Nothing" — a necessity made plain by the elusive zero-point energy. Quantum mathematicians know that unifying mathematics and physics was indispensable to the discoveries of quantum mechanics; a further union will be needed to build new systems of mathematics that bridge the gap between the world of the large and the world of the small.

Further still, the Grand Unified Theory will, in this researcher's view, have to be interdisciplinary to the degree that every branch of knowledge contributes — at once rational, empirical, and intuitive. There can be no Theory of Everything that excludes all but one thing; that would be a contradiction in terms. Mathematics is nature's language, but mathematics is not one thing — it is everything. It contains all the positive integers (1, 2, 3 … ∞) and all the negative integers (−1, −2, −3 … −∞), and the sum of all of them is 0. Hence mathematics is nil: nothing and everything. Every human system, too, can be read as a number — if theism is 1 and atheism is −1, the sum of the two is 0, the point of equilibrium. Cooperation on this scale would secure a leap in human knowledge unmatched in recorded history.

The ancients were well aware of these propositions; one need only look to Anaximander, the Buddha, Democritus, and Lao Tzu. Whether they called it the Empty, the Tao, the Infinite, or the One seems beside the point. There is, to be sure, a distinction between oneness and "none-ness" — unity usually denoting ordered manifestation, none-ness denoting voided chaos — yet the connecting factor at the substrate of reality permeates everything, whatever its place in the cosmic locality. The pre-Socratic philosophers anticipated the principles of modern physics in many ways, positing only atoms and the void. Older ideas need not be discarded, only modified to fit the progress of knowledge. No one should ever demand that a lover of wisdom suppress new knowledge in order to preserve an outmoded enterprise.

## Chapter Six: Conclusion

The archetype of the Absolute has been shown to be nothing and everything. To render it apprehensible — insofar as it can be grasped at all — requires a variety of disciplines and an abundance of perspectives. Philosophy, science, theology, mathematics, history, and mysticism have each been examined here to show that each holds a piece of the puzzle in solving the riddle of existence. Zero and infinity — nothing and everything — have been scrutinized, as have the positions of the foremost thinkers in their respective fields. The significance of an interdisciplinary study of being and nonbeing can scarcely be overstated.

Any ontological endeavor has the potential to benefit the one who undertakes it; and improvements to the inquirer's own character may, in turn, enrich the lives of those around them. On a larger scale, scientists — physicists above all — would gain enormously from cooperating with philosophers and metaphysicians. Quantum mechanics, after all, is by its very nature more metaphysical than the materialism and empiricism of the mainline sciences. One can marvel at the thought of the physicist and the philosopher discoursing on the authentic nature of being, devising testable hypotheses to learn whether their musings are correct. Such a partnership, however, must spring from a love of wisdom, knowledge, and truth. It will never come about so long as everyone remains shut within compartmentalized boxes built for perpetuity and profit. A new breed of philosopher-scientists must emerge, centered axiologically upon a love for the Real.

One can scarcely deny the mathematical nature of reality. One may take the view that mathematics is merely descriptive, yet observed reality behaves exactly like its numerical sibling. Mathematics therefore plays a profound role in revealing the undiluted nature of the universe — a point well worth watching in the years to come. The universal language is mathematical, and everyone speaks it: some as 1's, some as 2's, some as −1's and −2's. In any case, every individual is one among an infinity of others, holding one perspective amid an infinity of perspectives. Whether those numbers are potential or actual is of no consequence to finite beings. What matters is respect for the diversity that issues from the same source: absolutely nothing at all.
