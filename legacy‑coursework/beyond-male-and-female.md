# Beyond Male and Female: A Structural and Linguistic Analysis of the "Adam" Archetype

**Gerett Treas**

## Abstract

This paper investigates the etymological, theological, and metaphysical dimensions of the Hebrew term "adam" within the Genesis narrative. By analyzing linguistic roots (H430, H120, H6083) and integrating comparative theological perspectives, the study challenges the traditional singular-male interpretation of the primordial human. Instead, it presents adam as an androgynous or dual-natured being representing collective humanity and its intrinsic connection to the material world (adamah). Additional reflections from Jewish mysticism, New Testament usage, and modern scholarship highlight the inclusivity and complexity of the term. This analysis functions as a foundational precursor to later structural metaphysics, demonstrating early methodological continuity between linguistic-theological inquiry and the later formal development of Zenetist thought.

## Introduction

This paper examines the multifaceted nature of the term "adam" in the book of Genesis, exploring its linguistic roots, theological implications, and broader interpretive possibilities. Rather than approaching adam purely as an individual male figure, the study investigates how the Hebrew terminology, narrative context, and subsequent interpretive traditions reveal a more complex, inclusive, and layered concept of humanity. Through philological examination, comparative tradition analysis, and engagement with both classical and contemporary scholarship, the paper demonstrates that adam functions as a collective, dual-sexed, and earth-linked archetype whose meaning extends beyond conventional gender categories.

## The Linguistic Foundations of "Adam"

In Genesis 1:27, where the creation of adam is described, the word used for God is *'ĕlōhîm* (H430, אֱלֹהִים). This term is grammatically plural and can denote "gods," "divine ones," or "rulers," and may encompass both masculine and feminine dimensions. This plurality suggests that the "image" in which adam is created likewise contains both male and female aspects, opening interpretive space for androgyny or dual-sexuality within the original human.

A useful interpretive exercise is to imagine the term rendered as "humanity" instead of "man." Such a shift reframes the narrative by emphasizing collective identity rather than male individuality. The Hebrew term adam frequently carries this collective meaning, and this broader usage challenges the assumption that Genesis 1:27 describes a solely male being.

Genesis 2:7 continues this theme. Although often interpreted as the creation of a single male individual, the term used—H120, adam—does not linguistically necessitate maleness and can imply a dual-sexed nature [2]. The subsequent separation of Eve in Genesis 2:21–22 may therefore symbolize the division of the female aspect from an originally dual-sexed being rather than the creation of a separate sex ex nihilo.

## Earth-Connection and Embodiment

Genesis 2:7 depicts adam as formed from the "dust" (*'āp̄ār*, H6083) [3] of the ground (adamah). Both terms share a root connected to redness—possibly referencing blood for adam and red soil for adamah. This linguistic association underscores the intimate relationship between humanity and earth: humanity is not merely placed upon the earth but constituted from it.

The adam–adamah linkage also suggests that humanity's identity is inseparable from creation and ecology. This insight is amplified by New Testament usage: Luke 3:38 describes Adam as "the red earth" (G76) [4], reinforcing the symbolic connection between human identity and the material world.

## Dual-Sexed Interpretations and Jewish Mysticism

The notion that the primordial adam contained both male and female aspects is supported by Jewish mystical tradition. Kabbalistic teachings, as summarized in "The Female Aspect of Adam" on Chabad.org [6], describe the first human as a unified being later differentiated into male and female. This reinforces the idea that duality was a later emergence rather than an original condition.

## Adam as Collective Humanity

Peter Enns' analysis further distinguishes between adam as a universal designation for humanity and Adam as a specific representative within that collective [7]. This interpretation aligns with Genesis 5:2: "male and female created he them; and blessed them, and called their name Adam." Both sexes are explicitly encompassed under the single name Adam, affirming that the term expresses collective identity more than biological maleness.

This broader interpretive frame—common in ancient Jewish thought—emphasizes moral, spiritual, and communal identity over physical characteristics.

## Theological Implications for Gender and Humanity

The structural interpretation of adam challenges gender hierarchies embedded in later readings of Genesis. If adam originally represents humanity as a dual-sexed entity or as a collective whole, then traditional claims about male primacy lose textual support. Instead, the narrative suggests equal standing, mutuality, and shared essence.

The view that God encompasses both male and female aspects reinforces this egalitarian reading, offering a theological foundation for more inclusive and equitable understandings of human identity.

## Humanity, Earth, and Stewardship

Because adam is derived from adamah, this interpretation extends beyond human relationships to include the human-earth relationship. Humanity's origin from the soil implies stewardship, responsibility, and ecological reciprocity. In contemporary ecological crises, this ancient insight acquires renewed urgency.

## Artistic Reflections

Michelangelo's *The Creation of Adam* visually expresses the closeness between God and humanity. Although interpreted in various ways, its iconic depiction gestures toward the themes of shared essence, mirrored form, and potential androgyny discussed in this paper.

## Conclusion

The term "adam" in Genesis carries far richer and more expansive meaning than its common rendering as "man" suggests. Through linguistic analysis, comparative scholarship, and theological reflection, adam emerges as:

- a collective designation for humanity,
- a being embodying both male and female aspects,
- an entity intrinsically connected to the earth,
- and a symbol of humanity's intimate relationship with the divine.

By reclaiming this broader meaning, contemporary readers can access an ancient narrative that affirms dignity, equality, stewardship, and spiritual depth. Such an interpretation not only enriches biblical understanding but also fosters empathy, ecological responsibility, and a more harmonious conception of human identity. The Genesis depiction of adam remains a living narrative that continues to shape and illuminate our understanding of ourselves and our world.
