# Environmental Ethics: Monism, Pluralism, and Unity

**Gerett Treas**
PHI 376 — John Muenzberg
October 10, 2015

---

The clarity of J. Baird Callicott's position on moral monism, and of Christopher D. Stone's version of moral pluralism, exemplifies an issue of primary importance. The two positions represent contrasting ideological frameworks within environmental philosophy, and they seem to be irreconcilable systems in conflict with one another. To define them at the outset: monism holds that it is possible to promote an ethical constituency so large that it would encompass the entire world, while pluralism takes the stance that there is no single ethical principle, or set of principles, that could possibly fulfill such a global role (Light & Rolston, 2003, p. 3). The present aim is to lay out the monist and pluralist arguments of Callicott and Stone, to shed light on some recent developments in environmental ethics, and to add a further perspective to the views commonly held in this field. While seeking to give credit and respect to the dedicated men and women of this branch of philosophy, I will also call attention to what may be false, and in some cases potentially dangerous.

In "The Case Against Moral Pluralism," Callicott lays out his monist perspective in opposition to an earlier article by Stone. He begins by recounting his initial intention to create an ethic *of* the environment, rather than an ethic *for the use of* the environment. Stone, however, rejected the steps that followed in *Earth and Other Ethics: The Case for Moral Pluralism*, claiming that "[t]he Earth and other ethical requirements simply stretch any given moral theory to the breaking point" (Light & Rolston, 2003, p. 203). Stone felt that environmental ethics had, at the time, reached a plateau: the habitual call for reform, while poring over familiar themes, had reached a point of stagnation. Callicott does admit, however, that Stone put together "a frank, strong, and eminently reasonable case for moral pluralism" (Light & Rolston, 2003, p. 205).

In "Moral Pluralism and the Course of Environmental Ethics," Stone first relays his concern for the field and questions whether it has anything to show for itself. Upon reflection, he confirms that it does, reasoning that what environmental ethics has accomplished has been impressive: the basic issues had been clarified and the foundational obstacles addressed. This seems inconsistent with Callicott's assertion that Stone felt environmental ethics had reached a plateau — since, in Callicott's article, Stone is quoted as saying exactly that in one of his own works. In "Moral Pluralism," Stone first concerns himself with "the obstacles" that environmental ethicists had identified over the preceding ten years. One way he presents his account of monism is by saying that entities are either morally relevant or else utterly inconsiderable — out in the moral cold (Light & Rolston, 2003, p. 195).

Before delving deeper, it is worthwhile to look at some assertions made by Callicott and Stone, so that we may keep their stances in mind for the positions that follow. Callicott, following what is primarily a Western philosophical and religious view, asserts that, like humans, unconscious organisms compete for nutrients; they defend themselves in a variety of impressive ways, and even have their own good. Only sentient organisms, however, are aware of their strivings and feel urges and impulses. At the uppermost rung of the ladder of life is the human being, who is self-aware and has wishes, desires, hopes, and goals. Among other environmental philosophers, such as Taylor and Rolston, there is a further assignment of "double value," moving from the singularly conscious to the dually self-conscious. Views of this kind should be called into question in the future, though a proper rebuttal would take far more than the space presently allotted (Light & Rolston, 2003, p. 207).

One reason for choosing this issue as a topic is to point out the "othering" taking place — most likely unconsciously — in the field of environmental ethics at the time Callicott and Stone wrote their articles. Stone claims that the defense of animal rights has, in its arsenal, the facts that a life can be wiped out, a plan can be frustrated, and a nerve can transmit pain, whereas someone defending "inanimate" objects lacks a comparable basis for proving the intrinsic worth of something that cannot, as Stone puts it, be killed, frustrated, or pained (Light & Rolston, 2003, p. 194). Admittedly, things such as feeling and emotion are difficult to prove in entities that give no signs recognizable to the unaware human senses; and certainly many people who love animals from the bottom of their hearts care nothing for plants, for reasons of their own. The position of this paper is that the trouble with this mode of moral thought is its compartmentalization — the assumption that something either is or is not morally relevant, an either/or — when in fact such reasoning arises from thinking that is not innate. Dualistic thought develops in early childhood and manifests as a mindset of self and other: me, and other people, things, and objects. From this point, the categories are further subdivided.

Darren Domsky observed that Callicott's attack on Stone takes three forms: he argued that Stone's pluralism has no means of weighing and balancing conflicting duties; that such pluralism will tempt malicious individuals to make greedy decisions out of selfish tendencies; and that Stone's system is unable to provide a coherent ethical philosophy that is both contradiction-free and apparently true (Domsky, 2001, p. 411). Domsky also claimed that Callicott gave three reasons to reject pluralism. First, a pluralist is incapable of finding middle ground in the contradictory states of affairs created by his own system — because, when one is confronted with contradictory commands regarding forbidden actions, the implications of the contradictory mandates carry equal weight. Second, pluralism can lead an undisciplined person to admit the influence of convenience or self-interest into decisions about moral dilemmas. Third — and this Domsky considers most important — pluralism overlooks the need for a consistent moral philosophy, one that is constant and contradiction-free (Domsky, 2001, pp. 395–396).

A modern concept that might play a crucial role in environmental ethics — and, in turn, in the monist–pluralist debate between Callicott and Stone — is that of global citizenship and global community, which arguably can be applied universally to all life, human and nonhuman. A global community implies, of course, that there is one community; yet that one community is composed of individual global citizens who together form a unitary body. This conveys the idea that we are one among many, and many within one. True reality is already unity in diversity. In *Environmental Ethics: An Overview for the Twenty-First Century*, Robin Attfield claims that today's many environmental problems call for a global, cosmopolitan ethic, and for its recognition as such by all who consider themselves global citizens (Attfield, 2014). Attfield also calls for participation in a developing global community to address shared challenges. This paper agrees with these proposals. Problems arise, as they always do, when compartmentalization begins.

It is recognized that obligations must not cease at national boundaries. Attfield, in fact, cites Leopold as suggesting that human beings are not the only moral agents capable of global citizenry. Attfield then rebuts this idea by noting that citizens have political and cultural responsibilities that plants and non-human animals do not. This, however, is far too narrow a view of universality — one in which the human being is but a single entity in a vast sea of innumerable entities (Attfield, 2014, chap. 6). Man has assumed that he is superior because he can build shopping malls, rally troops for battle, and domesticate (enslave) "lesser" entities to his own ends. Perhaps animals and plants simply are not concerned with these direly important tasks; perhaps they are occupied instead with living life as humans should. Indeed, animals and plants live far closer to the ideal life taught by all the great religious and philosophical traditions — in the here and now, with a still mind, free of attachment and desire.

In "Biodiversity and Environmental Values: In Search of a Universal Earth Ethic," Bryan G. Norton offers another alternative to monism: "adaptive management." This has arisen out of progress in understanding environmental ethics and the problems it contains in adapting to complex, multiscalar, dynamic systems. The result may become the dominant management approach of the twenty-first century, taking a more holistic and process-oriented view of conservation (Norton, 2000, p. 1030). Adaptive managers argue that landscape ecological systems often become "brittle" when continually exploited, and that large systems can diminish or re-stabilize at different levels of functioning. They likewise view incidents as "cross-scale spillovers," meaning that large-scale abuse of resources will have broad-scale consequences for various aspects of the environment (Norton, 2000, p. 1035). The question the managers pose to the monists is this (Norton, 2000, p. 1041): if we no longer need to sort entities into those of instrumental and those of intrinsic value, why do we need monism? Thus the lines blur where sharp ethical contrasts are concerned.

Alternative solutions to the positions of Callicott and Stone are not necessary; some general refinements and the principle of non-exclusivism are all that is needed. Insofar as Callicott wishes to see a single ethical theory, while Stone believes there is no one feature, subject, or principle fit to measure all ethical activity, a mediating solution may be sought that incorporates the highest aspects of both views. Here "highest" indicates only the positive pole of a polarity viewed dualistically. Beyond such dualism is unity — the state in which there can be no conflict.

The reference work *Green Ethics and Philosophy: An A-to-Z Guide* states that "[e]thical pluralism seems especially suited to environmental ethics, as moral diversity can be conceived as part of or at least analogous to Earth's biotic diversity" (Newman & Robbins, 2011, p. 198). This paper applies pressure against all assertions of — and especially all reverence for — "diversity," the meaning and implications of which should be properly understood. In *Lectures on Ancient Philosophy: Companion to The Secret Teachings of All Ages*, Manly P. Hall asserts:

> Diversity is the paramount illusion. Only realization can overcome the concept of diversity… That which man believes he knows he has divided into a host of subdivisions, each of which is served by intellects naturally segregative and separative. The physicist, the biologist, and the anthropologist all live in worlds apart, each seeking with his own particular fragment of the body of knowledge to solve the riddle of it all. However, as consciousness reveals more and more of Reality to man, all these man made divisions disappear and the multitude of streamlets converge to form the three great rivers of science, philosophy, and religion. These, in turn, eventually empty into the one great ocean of Truth. (Hall, 2005, p. 118)

This excerpt does not deny that differences exist; it denies only that they are exclusive, since all belong to "one great ocean of Truth." Diversity exists only insofar as it is fragmentarily unified. A physicist, a biologist, and an anthropologist — to use Hall's examples — can each function in their particular areas, so long as they are not separative; but it must be recognized that each is only one cog in the same machine, his knowledge only a fragment of universal truth. When the illusion of subdivision ceases, knowledge will merge and understanding will expand exponentially. No one would deny that the ocean has its streamlets and droplets of water; similarly, one should not deny that all the shards of the shattered mirror of truth have their actuality as one undivided portion of a whole. What is needed is non-separation and non-segregation. Each field of human endeavor should realize its part in the working of the whole — environmentally, culturally, societally, and totally, all in all.

Bringing this line of thought to bear on the Stone–Callicott debate should make it easy to see that argument is not really necessary. Noble intentions are no excuse for stubbornness and uncooperativeness. Rather than argue, the two should relate — or better, acknowledge that they are already related. No aspect of nature should be subdivided into a category that makes it somehow "other." According to the teachings of Pythagoras, the Monad, or One, represents the principle of Unity from which all things arise; the Dyad, or Two, represents Duality and the beginning of multiplicity, struggle, and strife. The Monad may, however, shine its light through the Dyad and bring forth the Triad, offering the possibility of reconciliation to all things divided (Guthrie, 1988, p. 21).

Given the spatial limitations of this paper, it is impossible to explore every angle, issue, and viable solution. One thing is certain: this is not merely an environmental issue but a universal one — further evidence that the subfield of environmental ethics, however noble, is still limited by its own exclusivity. This ethic is a product of human conception, imagination, and practice, and it ought not to be localized, segregated, or compartmentalized. Universals do not conflict with particulars; they ought rather to take precedence over them. Also troublesome is the task of satisfactorily explaining Callicott's and Stone's positions while simultaneously offering alternative interpretations. It has proven sufficient here to state the basic standings of Callicott and Stone, to give some modern criticisms and solutions in the field of environmental ethics, and to offer an interpretation that could one day lead toward the unity of all beings. Here "all" means the entire universe — not what we have divided and subdivided into categories and subcategories on the basis of our current and limited understanding. The future will be bright if the pride of strong identification — not only with human types and groups, such as gender and race, but likewise with species and kinds, such as animal, plant, and mineral — gives way, so that all are respected and considered intrinsically valuable parts of a single universal body endowed with soul and spirit. In the state of unity, there can be no conflict. To this end, the invisible kingdom is being built beyond the purview of those who are transcendentally veiled.
