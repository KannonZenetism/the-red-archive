# Quakers and Gnostics: Inward Light of Intuitive Knowing

**Gerett Treas**  
RGS350-01 / HIS390-01  
April 10, 2015

---

Modern Christianity borrowed a great deal from ancient thought, and understanding this is of primary importance. A paper of this length can only partly uncover such deeply hidden truths, but it aims to illuminate as many of them as possible. The present work focuses on two strikingly similar movements within Christianity, one ancient and one modern. The Gnostics — or what might otherwise be called "Mystic Christians" — helped to form the basis of modern Christian belief, and the Society of Friends, otherwise known as the "Quakers," hold strikingly similar teachings. A line of descent runs from the ancient world to the modern, carrying with it a mystical process by which, its adherents believe, one may come to know God — not in the orthodox manner, through written texts and formal doctrine, but through something that comes from within. The Gnostics called this "gnosis"; the Quakers called it the "Inner Light."

To begin with, one should consider the historical roots of these two movements. Gnosticism has its roots in the Second Temple period and counted Greeks, Romans, and Jews among its adherents. It was not limited to Christianity, being a more pervasive, inclusive, and universal system of practice than any form of orthodoxy. There were Gnostics from Iraq, Iran, Europe, the Middle East, North America, and China. Unlike any rigid structure of belief, Gnosticism was a meshing of many different ideas drawn from various systems. Gnostics sought wisdom wherever it could be found, so long as it corresponded to the knowing contained within their own persons. They were known to study not only Jewish and Christian texts, but also Greco-Roman philosophy, the religious documents of Egypt and Mesopotamia, and the teachings of the Zoroastrians, Muslims, and Buddhists. As one can see, they were varied and diverse — perhaps more so, in this respect, than the Quakers.

The Quakers arose in England around the middle of the seventeenth century. Many civil and religious upheavals of the time broke down the bonds of priestcraft and prepared the way for new and innovative spiritual disciplines. The suppression of the human mind was diminishing as people's dependence upon the church decreased, leading to a more personal kind of Christianity. George Fox, the first Quaker, was considered anointed — one sent into the world to preach a new revelation of the gospel. Fox was born at Drayton, in Leicestershire, in 1624. His parents educated him, and he was raised Episcopalian, but he eventually withdrew from the church and spent much of his time reading the scriptures on his own. He desired that God reveal to his spirit how rightly to understand the message of Christ; and when the revelation came, he became aware of the corruption that had seeped into the church of his day. He became a foot-traveling preacher in England who refused to accept payment for his humble services, and within a few years many people had adopted his way of understanding biblical teaching (Evans, 1870, pp. 7–10).

William Penn was a highly important figure in the Quaker movement. Not only was he the founder of the colony of Pennsylvania, or "Penn's Woods," he was also a close friend of George Fox and a propagator of the "new gospel" when the movement first began. Penn was very young at the time of his conversion: while attending Oxford, he came to realize that much of the simplicity had been taken out of the fundamental basis of Christianity. He began attending Quaker meetings, and in doing so found that he was not the only one of his peers to share such dissatisfaction with the prevailing religious attitude of the time. So Penn and the others withdrew from the official, organized, ceremonial worship and began to hold their own meetings, much to the dismay of the authorities. Eventually, because of his refusal to renounce such practices, he was expelled from the college — which distressed his father terribly and made him fear for any chance his son might have at worldly honor (Janney, 1851, p. 16).

Penn exchanged a good many letters with George Fox, as can be found in the book *The Papers of William Penn*. In a letter to Fox written on the 7th of September, 1674, Penn exalts his presumed teacher in righteousness thus: "In the unalterable love of god, I salute thee, & my spirit visits thee, whom I Cannot but remember with all endeared Affection…the lord god preserve peace in his church & a sound & plain heart in friends amen" (Penn, 1981, p. 291). He greets Fox with the utmost joy, but there is a substantial amount of unstated doctrine wrapped within the closing phrase, "sound & plain heart." Since the Quakers rejected ideas of authority, titles, fancy dress, or anything that set one thing over or apart from another, the plain heart proved to be a cornerstone of what they understood to be humility in living — something they took extremely seriously (Gummere, 1968, p. 1).

With limited space, this paper cannot cover all the various sects or founders of the orders of Gnosticism — to say nothing of the impossibility of finding the original Gnostic — so the focus will rest on only one. The Prophet Mani was in fact one of the later founders of a Gnostic sect, but it was highly syncretic, incorporating Christian, Zoroastrian, and Buddhist concepts. Manichaeism was one of the major world religions of its time, the span of which coincided with Mani's life (216–277 CE), and it was the only such major world religion to align itself with the late Near Eastern Gnostic tradition. Mani was born in Mesopotamia, but his parents were of Iranian lineage. He entered a Jewish-Christian baptismal sect at the age of four. At twelve he had a vision of an angel he called "the twin," who called him to leave the baptismal sect at some later, unspecified date. He had another and more important vision at twenty-four, when the angel ordered him to begin a public ministry preaching his new doctrine. Mani proclaimed himself a messenger of light and regarded all other religions as contaminated versions of what Buddha, Jesus, and Zoroaster had originally taught. Essentially, Mani was another case of the rejection of institutionalized religion (Barnstone & Meyer, 2009, pp. 589–600).

The most sensible thing to do when dealing with a historical process — the attempt to trace a thing's roots from antiquity to modernity — is to begin with the former and then move to the latter. With that in mind, we must bring Gnosticism (which in this paper may be used interchangeably with Mystic Christianity) under an observant eye. Gnosticism is somewhat like other spiritual "systems" in that it functions as a collective method for those who possess what is termed *gnosis*. Gnosis is a Greek word meaning "knowledge," but this is not just any knowledge; it is "intuitive knowledge acquired from within oneself and one's own mystical experience" (Malachi, 2005, p. 2). One may also consider it "knowing directly," as if one were in the presence of God — not in the body, but in the spirit. At the outset, the contradistinction between intuitive knowledge and belief, commonly called "faith," should be explained. Knowers have no need of faith, since their knowledge derives from the Source in a way that leaves them lacking nothing and so needing to believe in nothing unproven; the knower already has sufficient evidence within. It is this knowledge that others merely believe in.

Now, for our benefit, we must look introspectively into the circumstances that gave rise to these incredibly deep yet mostly rejected forms of Christianity; the reason for such rejection will eventually reveal itself. Gnosticism, generally speaking, predates Christianity by thousands of years. Part of the mystical doctrines of Egypt, Mesopotamia, and Greece, gnosis permeates many cultures. In these settings, the schools of gnosis represented ineffable spiritual mysteries given to, or received by, only the "elect." There were pagan forms of Gnosticism, as well as a speculative degree of influence from Buddhist and Hindu sources — and vice versa. In the beginning, ancient Christianity was a wholly oral tradition, never committed to writing. It was immensely diverse, and the precision of the teaching depended more upon the receptivity of the disciple than upon the priest and the clergy. The entire point of the movement was the transmission of these inward, heart-level teachings, conveying the reality of the Christ presence within the individual — just like the Inward Light of the Quakers.

Both of these forms of Mystic Christianity were persecuted by the established orthodoxy of the time, though the Gnostics far more violently. Practicing Gnostics were put to death by the early Church, even though, before it became an official state institution, Gnosticism had formed the inner circle of the outer church. In other words, Gnosticism was connected to the exoteric church as it appeared in the religion of the masses; the more advanced souls would peer deeper into the workings of the spirit, increasing their level of spirituality and so, in effect, becoming Gnostics. But this would not last terribly long, for under the Roman Empire these early Christians began to be put to death. It is a little-known fact, well kept from the modern church, that early Christians such as the Gnostics and the Essenes were practicing vegetarians. Here is how such "evil" was dealt with under the "holy" rule of the Romans:

> Even after Literalist Christianity had become the state religion of the Roman Empire, Gnosticism remained a powerful force. In the fourth century heretical Christians were still so common that Cyril of Jerusalem had to warn the faithful to be careful not to step into a Gnostic church by mistake. In the reign of Theodosius there were so many heretics among the clergy and monks in Egypt that the Patriarch Timothy made eating meat compulsory on Sundays, to flush out the vegetarian Gnostics! (Freke & Gandy, 1999, p. 246)

The Quakers' persecution mostly consisted of imprisonment and estrangement against their largely Puritan backdrop. One cited reason for their confinement was their supposed disturbance of church services. On top of this, they were charged with blasphemy, plotting against the government, and contempt of court — all of which are possible, but most of them unlikely. Quakerism existed in a strongly Puritan environment, and it is, sadly, the history of religious thought to be highly intolerant toward those who are other, or different from the norm. Quakers often felt they received commands from the divine, a natural effect of possessing the Inward Light; and when such a thing happened, nothing — not even the Puritanical punishments — could stop them. Nor was it made any easier for the Quakers by the fact that they felt called, just as the Hebrew prophets had been, to carry out divinely inspired actions, such as taking dangerous journeys overseas or even appearing half-naked as a sign in a marketplace. History does show us that religious growth and devotion happen more fervently under strong persecution than in times of peace (Lucas, 1949, pp. 65–71).

The concept of God also varies between our two venerable classifications of spirituality. The Quakers still seemed to view the God of the Old Testament as the Heavenly Father of Jesus Christ, even though the Tetragrammaton YHWH — often pronounced "Yahweh" — is nowhere used by Christ in any of the four gospel accounts. Christ refers to his Father in an odd context only once, when he was on the cross and uttered, "Eli, Eli, why hast thou forsaken me?" — that is, "My God, My God, why hast thou forsaken me?" This is actually a quite un-Yahwistic statement. Nevertheless, Fox and the Quakers do not make this distinction — which is just as well, since they took away any such authority by turning inward upon themselves. In George Fox's letters, his 2nd Epistle states: "The children of God are pure in heart, not looking only at the outside. The favor of the world and friendship with it is enmity to God… For those who followed Christ in his cross, they were strangers in the world" (Worthington, n.d.). Hence the distinction drawn between any God merely of the world and the authorities working on such a God's behalf.

The Gnostic concept of the Christian Creator God is one of the foremost things that earned them so much hatred. Being famed "world-haters," the Gnostics were inclined not to concede that the created world was a good thing. They chose instead to see the creation as flawed — as it really is, if some degree of observation is given to the proposition. Since the creation is flawed, they reasoned, the Creator must be flawed as well; reason would have it that only a flawed creator could produce a flawed creation. But is the Creator God the sole God of the cosmos in Gnostic thinking? Absolutely not. The Prime Creator is referred to by different names, by different sects of Gnostics from different parts of the world, but always with the idea in mind that the Absolute is undefinable — lest He-She be called the All, the Ineffable, the Unutterable, the Unsearchable, or even, in a slightly Platonic light, simply the Good.

The Creator God of the Gnostics is called the demiurge, meaning "half-maker," since this being never really brought anything into existence on its own but only created out of what was already there. The demiurge is in fact a concept drawn from Plato, and the Gnostics knew this being by various other names: Ahriman, El, Saklas, Samael, Satan, Yaldabaoth, or Yahweh (Barnstone & Meyer, 2009). It is easy to see how such a teaching could have enraged the early church fathers — though not to the point of committing murder. Careful examination of the biblical texts, heavily altered and corrupted as they are, can still yield plenty of truths that point in this direction. The Secret Book of John makes a distinction between what is called the One and Yaldabaoth. Of the One it says: "It is the god and father of all, the invisible one that is over all, that is incorruptible, that is pure light at which no eye can gaze. The One is the invisible spirit" (Barnstone & Meyer, 2009, p. 159). In contrast, of the Creator God, here called Yaldabaoth, it says: "Yaldabaoth is the first ruler, who took great power from his mother…He is wicked in his mindlessness that is in him. He said, I am god and there is no other god but me, since he did not know where his strength came from" (Barnstone & Meyer, 2009, pp. 166–167). The latter part of the statement — "there is no other god but me" — should be easily recognizable as belonging to the God of the Hebrew Old Testament.

Because this paper aims for clarity between the traditions, we will now turn to the fundamental aspects of the Quakers that make them so much like the Gnostics and other earlier mystical Christian sects. The unique position of the Quakers among the forms of modern Christianity is their belief in the "Inward Light," which should at once strike a familiar chord, given how gnosis has just been described. This Inward Light is the center of the Quaker way, the axis upon which all their other doctrines turn. As such, the view of it stood "behind all the structure of organization, and at the heart of the Quaker faith," and, "shining dimly in some and more strongly with others, it was identified with the Light that shone supremely in Jesus" (Lucas, 1949, p. 48). This light was a form of illumination occurring within a person in the form of Jesus. In this respect the Gnostic might lean more toward a belief in the "Inner Christ," which is substantially different, since the Gnostic view of the Christ spirit, or logos, is not innately identified with the person of Jesus. Many Gnostics hold that the Christ spirit descended upon him at his baptism by John in the river Jordan and departed from him before his death upon the cross at Golgotha.

Much the same could not be said of the Quakers. While they accepted scripture as coming from holy, or enlightened, men, it did come from *men*; and so the best way for them to obtain knowledge of God was through the Inward Light:

> By exclusive dependence on the written word of the Bible, their contemporaries had lost much of the life and stimulus in religion. Quakers turned from the outward authority of the Scriptures to an inward authority, believing that in every man was something of God which, like an Inward Light, would lead toward Truth and Goodness. This belief in an Inward Light—the Light, they held, that had inspired the writers of the Scriptures and was present in some measure in all men—this was the source of power expressed in the Quaker message. The Spirit behind the words of the Bible was greater than the words themselves, and was still in men to inspire and empower them. (Lucas, 1949, pp. 25–26)

One thing the Quakers have more in common with modern Christianity is the acknowledgment of at least some authority in scripture. Where the Quaker would read the scriptures as coming from enlightened men, the Gnostic would take them as symbolic representations of things happening on the level of human consciousness. Even so, the detachment of sole authority from the scriptures, and its reassignment to something that restores personal authority, is distinctly missing from the religions of the world and is shared between these two. It would be folly, however, to consider Gnosticism a religion rather than a spiritual practice.

These two similarities alone are enough to make the astute thinker curious: How did the originator of the Quakers, George Fox, arrive at his conclusion? Was he a self-realized Gnostic who, through self-seeking, came to the reality of inner gnosis, or did he perhaps learn of some ancient teaching along his path to enlightenment? The research is not entirely conclusive on this point, but what is certain is that there is a clear and distinct mystical tinge to Mr. Fox's brand of Christianity. Quakerism grew out of a distaste for religion as it was in the founder's day. George Fox was wholly dissatisfied with the limiting effect the religiosity of the time had upon religious practice. As one report has it, "The fetters, in which the priestcraft had long held the human mind, were beginning to be loosened" (Evans, 1870, p. 7). It is in this way that one's inward realization mattered, for it was this that freed the Friends from the dogmatic rules and regulations imposed upon them.

An outstanding aspect of Gnosticism is, as already mentioned, its stress on direct knowing — the intuitive experience of the presence of God on a personal, intimate level. The Gnostic does not need ritual or ceremony to obtain this kind of enlightenment, though many have practiced them as they liked. It is a sort of seed that wells up in the self, a self-realized manifestation of an inward growth. To the orthodox this often seems appalling — that one might not sing praises to one's Lord. Yet whereas the Literalist views external ceremony in the structural temple as the way to connect with God, the Gnostic views internal connection within the bodily temple as the way of merging with God. It is a difference in perspective. In a Gnostic reading of scripture, for example, one might say that they read white while the orthodox read black: the two are reading the same material, only reading it differently.

Along with this, the Gnostics had their own body of scriptures. There is a compilation of the texts into what is known as the Gnostic Bible, but the most commonly referenced texts are part of the Nag Hammadi Library, where these ancient sacred texts were rediscovered. The thirteen codices of the Nag Hammadi library were excavated in 1945; there were also discoveries of documents known as the Berlin Gnostic Codex 8502, around thirty years earlier, and, more recently, the Codex Tchacos. These texts contain alternative gospel accounts such as the Gospel of Thomas and the Gospel of Mary. "The Nag Hammadi Scriptures includes nearly fifty texts that were read as sacred literature and gathered in collection found near Nag Hammadi and elsewhere in Egypt…" (Meyer, 2007, p. 1). So, alongside the canonical gospels of the New and Old Testaments, the Gnostics also had their own documents — never really meant to be taken literally, but allegorically, as inner explorations of consciousness that would lead the individual onto the path of gnosis. By comparison, the Quakers had no such unique scripture of their own, which, strangely enough, seems to make them more Gnostic than the Gnostics in this regard — though, as already noted, the Gnostic texts were meant only as guides, not as any kind of formal authority.

There were some variations among the founders of the Gnostics and Quakers as to what they taught and believed. In Gnosticism the variations occurred in the larger constructs of thought; in Quakerism the differences arose only between individuals, as with George Fox compared to William Penn, his successor. In both schools of thought, the greatest emphasis was placed not on outward but on inward authority. The Quaker would stress the guidance of the Light in accordance with the god-man Jesus, while the Gnostic stressed the realization of the self through the unveiling of the Christ within. The Jesus of the Quakers is not necessarily the Christ of the Gnostics. Many Gnostics, though not all, believe that the spirit of the Christ descended upon the Master Jesus while he was alive and left him while he was on the cross during his crucifixion. This is why many have considered them heretics: their doctrine of Jesus held that their God was above and beyond this world and would therefore not fully incarnate in human flesh. That God could become man and instruct men was no problem for the Quakers.

For the rest of the available space, the prospect of these spiritual systems will be discussed more broadly. The question is, do these ideas promoted by the mystical minds of their adherents have any value today? How can we learn from what they did, both right and wrong? Is there perhaps a further revelation to come in the future, or a return to the ancient wisdom necessary for the development of our world? These questions are important, because this is a time in history when many things are changing dramatically. The Quakers, for example, believed not in a physical second coming of Jesus, but in a coming of him within the person of the believer. It is not a matter of external forces, but of internal connection — that transformative change which cannot happen through ritual and dogma, that gnosis which can only come by revelation of the Inward Light. The Quakers held that the physical return of Christ, along with the complete destruction of the world, were not future matters of fact. Instead, they believed that the second coming is a revealing within the hearts of individual believers who have prepared themselves for it: the light hidden within becomes manifest, making the previously unseen visible. This is, evidently, the Quaker view of the destiny of the world. They believed that Jesus's return had already happened, and would continue to happen, for any adherent receptive enough to build up the light necessary for his manifestation within them. This is strikingly similar to the Gnostic view of ultimate salvation — a period of enlightenment for all mankind, when humanity attains such enlightenment that the presence of evil is dissipated.

The Gnostic view of salvation depends largely on the "seed" from which one comes. In the thought of Valentinus, an early and renowned Gnostic, the world is divided into three parts: the material, the psychical, and the spiritual. So too, he reasoned, are people: "Human beings, then, are divided into three groups in Valentinian thought: hylics—material people of flesh and blood who are unbelievers; psychics—psychical people, people with soul, who are ordinary Christians; and pneumatics—truly spiritual people, people with divine spirit, who are gnostics" (Barnstone & Meyer, 2009, p. 114). The last of these, the Valentinians reckoned, were themselves. The seed concept comes from a complex ideology concerning the bloodlines of early humanity. Since Cain had killed Abel, Adam and Eve had a second son, Seth, who was to be a son of promise. To this day, the descendants of Cain are "builders of cities," or edifiers of the material creation, while the descendants of Seth are thought to be the true bloodline of the light, on the side of the righteous God.

> The elect will become angels, the auditors will be judged righteous, and the sinners will be cast into hell with the sexually promiscuous woman Eve and her hellish offspring. The cosmos will disintegrate and burn in a conflagration for 1,468 years. The remaining light particles will be gathered and finally return by ascent to the kingdom of light, while the prince of darkness and his diabolical minions will be cast into a bottomless pit permanently sealed with a huge stone for all eternity. The two principles of light and darkness will again be two separate and distinct entities, never again to commingle. (Barnstone & Meyer, 2009, p. 598)

One might find this very out of character and unique — not only within Gnosticism, since Manichaeism is only one branch of gnosis, but within the Mystery traditions in general, which generally speak of unity and oneness. The author prefers to keep to the concept of oneness, as it is a less bleak outlook on the future. At face value such a statement would be irrelevant to this paper, but it helps to show the variation that existed among the various ancient Gnostic sects. And we can never forget that, for the Gnostic, it was always the inner meaning of the words that was significant — things were rarely as they appeared.
